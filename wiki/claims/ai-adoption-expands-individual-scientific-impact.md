## Statement

Scientists who adopt AI tools in their research produce more publications, accumulate more citations, and advance their careers faster than non-adopters. However, this individual-level productivity boost comes at a collective cost: AI adoption is associated with a narrowing of the research domains explored, as AI tools automate established (data-rich) fields rather than enabling frontier exploration of new ones.

## Evidence summary

Hao et al. (2025, Nature) analysed ~41M natural science papers (1980–2025), ~311,000 AI-augmented. AI adopters are more highly cited and publish in higher-impact journals across machine learning, deep learning, and generative AI eras. Simultaneously, AI adopters show contraction in knowledge focus — they research more topics but within fewer fields. Commentary by Storey (2025, Nature N&V) contextualises this as a paradox.

## Conditions and scope

- Natural sciences only; 1980–2025 dataset
- Three AI eras analysed separately: ML (1990s), DL (2010s), GenAI (2020s)
- Selection effects not fully controlled: more productive scientists may self-select into AI adoption and domain specialisation simultaneously
- Post-2022 generative AI era is under-represented in the dataset

## Counter-evidence

- No direct counter-evidence yet in the literature
- Gao & Wang (2024, Nature Human Behaviour) find AI adoption increases "hit paper" production but note strong inequality in who benefits — partially consistent

## Open questions

- Does narrowing persist as generative AI matures and lowers barriers to cross-domain work?
- Is this pattern specific to natural sciences or does it generalise?
- What is the mechanism: do AI tools preferentially work better in data-rich domains, or do scientists simply follow AI capabilities?
