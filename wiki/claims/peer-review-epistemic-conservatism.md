## Statement

Peer review, as the dominant quality-control mechanism in science and publicly-funded research programs, enforces epistemic conservatism: it can only evaluate new work relative to the existing stock of knowledge, systematically rewards incremental contributions over paradigm-challenging ones, and — when embedded in funding allocation — effectively removes support from unconventional high-risk projects.

## Evidence summary

Two complementary perspectives support this claim. Smith (2006) provides empirical evidence of peer review's low reliability, systematic biases against unknown authors and non-Western researchers, and inability to detect fraud — suggesting the system stabilizes existing power structures rather than purely rewarding quality. Knie (2026) extends this to the innovation-blocking function: in German federal funding programs, peer review by academic institutes acts as a gatekeeper that penalizes "too far from the existing state of knowledge," preventing the open-ended entrepreneurial research that produces breakthrough technologies.

## Conditions and scope

- The claim is strongest when peer review directly controls funding (DFG grants, federal program approvals), weaker in post-publication contexts
- The mechanism is structural, not intentional: reviewers are not trying to block innovation but evaluating by the only standard they have
- Context-dependent: fields with faster technological change (software, AI) may suffer less because the "existing stock" turns over quickly

## Counter-evidence

- Peer review has also enabled the review and diffusion of genuinely novel work (e.g., many Nobel Prize-winning papers passed peer review)
- Some funding schemes (e.g., ERC Advanced Grants, ARPA models) explicitly reward unconventional proposals — showing the conservatism is not inherent to all peer review but to specific institutional configurations

## Open questions
