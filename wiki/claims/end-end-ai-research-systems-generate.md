## Statement

Fully autonomous AI research systems can produce scientific manuscripts that pass initial peer review at ML conference workshops, without human authorship involvement in the research or writing process.

## Evidence summary

The AI Scientist (Lu et al., Nature 2026) is the primary evidence: 70% workshop acceptance rate for AI-generated manuscripts. This is the first system demonstrated in a real peer-review setting.

## Conditions and scope

- ML workshops have lower acceptance bars than main conference tracks or top journals
- Self-review in the pipeline creates a circular validation concern
- Tasks are ML-specific; generalization to experimental or theoretical sciences is untested

## Counter-evidence

- 30% rejection rate indicates AI-generated research does not universally pass peer review
- Risk of flooding review systems if widely adopted (not a counter to the claim per se, but a deployment concern)

## Linked ideas

## Open questions

- Does the acceptance rate hold at main conference tracks or top journals?
- How does reviewer skepticism about AI authorship affect outcomes?
