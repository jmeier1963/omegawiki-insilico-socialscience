## Problem

AI tools are rapidly adopted in scientific research with claims of expanding human understanding. But do AI tools actually expand scientific understanding, or do they create illusions of understanding while the underlying epistemic work is offloaded to machines?

## Key idea

AI tools in science create "illusions of understanding" because they perform cognitive labor that normally builds understanding. When AI automates literature review, hypothesis generation, data analysis, or interpretation, scientists believe they comprehend more than they do. This fosters scientific monocultures, reduces methodological diversity, and makes science more vulnerable to systematic errors.

## Method

- Conceptual/theoretical analysis drawing on philosophy of science and cognitive science
- Maps the stages of the research cycle where AI tools can create illusions
- Uses psychology of motivated reasoning and cognitive biases to explain uptake
- Published in *Nature* 627:49–58, 2024 (DOI 10.1038/s41586-024-07146-0)

## Results

- Four types of AI illusions: AI tools that seduce (too appealing to ignore), tools that overwhelm (so much output scientists can't critically evaluate), tools that organize (structuring their thinking around AI outputs), tools that validate (confirming existing beliefs)
- Monoculture risk: if all researchers use the same AI tools, the diversity of approaches decreases
- Not primarily a problem of individual researchers but of the social epistemic structure of science

## Limitations

- Theoretical analysis — empirical evidence for the proposed mechanisms is limited
- Conflates different types of AI tools and different stages of research
- May underestimate scientists' ability to maintain critical distance

## Open questions

- Empirically, do AI-assisted scientists have worse meta-knowledge (knowing what they don't know)?
- Can disclosure norms (DFG 2023) reduce illusions, or do they address the wrong level?
- How do Messeri/Crockett concerns interact with de Regt's (2017) contextual theory of understanding?

## My take

The most influential philosophical critique of AI in science. The "monoculture" concept is particularly important — even if each individual AI-assisted scientist gets smarter, if they all think the same way, science becomes collectively more fragile. This is a systems-level concern that individual-level AI evaluation misses. Well worth reading alongside Sullivan (2022) on understanding from ML models.
