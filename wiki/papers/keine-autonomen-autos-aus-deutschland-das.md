## Problem

Germany has no competitive autonomous vehicle company — no German equivalent of Waymo, Uber AV, Tesla, Pony.ai, or Baidu. Why does a country with a world-class automotive industry and a large academic research infrastructure fail to produce breakthrough mobility innovations? The paper argues the problem is structural, not technical.

## Key idea

Germany's inability to develop autonomous vehicles (and by extension transformative mobility innovations) results from a toxic combination of three mutually reinforcing institutional mechanisms: (1) academic peer review enforces epistemic conservatism by evaluating novelty only relative to existing knowledge, (2) the "Daseinsvorsorge" (public service obligation) logic of public transport prevents profitable entrepreneurial models and thus genuine risk-taking, and (3) hyper-specific regulation (AFGBV) sets standards for autonomous vehicles so high that deployment is practically impossible.

## Method

Commentary / opinion piece by the outgoing head of the WZB research group "Digital Mobility and Social Differentiation." Five years of empirical observation of the German mobility innovation landscape. No formal methods or quantitative data — analytical argument drawing on institutional theory and case observation.

- Published: WZB Mitteilungen, Heft 191, March 2026
- Author: Andreas Knie (WZB / TU Berlin, Chief Scientific Officer, Choice GmbH)

## Results

- Germany had 100+ autonomous vehicle projects, all in different fragmented formats, none reaching commercial scale
- The AFGBV requires autonomous vehicles to meet a much higher safety standard than existing human-driven systems — while 300,000+ people are injured annually in conventional road traffic, resulting in zero autonomous vehicle deployments as of early 2026
- Academic research institutes function as gatekeepers in federal funding programs: unconventional projects with open-ended outcomes receive negative peer review evaluations, losing public funding
- Public transit is treated as a public good unable to make profits, which removes entrepreneurial incentives and results in prototype-level products with no scaling

## Limitations

- No empirical quantitative data — purely argumentative
- Comparison to US/Chinese companies (Waymo, Baidu) may not account for differences in regulatory environments, capital markets, and patent landscapes
- Author has a conflict of interest as a startup CSO (Choice GmbH) that advocates for platform mobility
- Does not address whether Germany's approach has prevented accidents (the paper itself acknowledges zero AV injuries, but dismisses this as evidence of non-deployment rather than safety achievement)

## Open questions

- Is Germany's innovation deficit in mobility a sector-specific failure or indicative of broader structural problems?
- Would loosening the AFGBV actually lead to commercial AV deployment, or are other barriers (trust, liability, infrastructure) more binding?
- Can the Daseinsvorsorge logic be reformed without abandoning the public good rationale for public transport?
- How does this analysis connect to debates about peer review reform and open science?

## My take

A provocative diagnosis from an insider. The peer-review-as-epistemic-conservatism argument is the most intellectually interesting piece — it connects to broader STS debates (Merton, Knorr-Cetina) about how scientific institutions stabilize rather than challenge paradigms. The Daseinsvorsorge critique is institutionally sharp: public service logic really does create a structural incompatibility with venture-scale risk. The weakest part is the regulatory critique — the argument that safety standards are "too high" for AVs while 300k people are injured in conventional traffic is a valid point, but Knie doesn't engage with the asymmetric liability and trust dynamics that make this comparison incomplete. As a commentary from a WZB group finale, it reads as a candid institutional post-mortem rather than a measured empirical study.
