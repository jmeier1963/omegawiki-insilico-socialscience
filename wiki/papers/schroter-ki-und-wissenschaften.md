## Problem

Sweeping claims about "AI transforming science" treat science as monolithic. But the sciences are highly differentiated. How does AI's impact actually vary across disciplines, and what does this mean for how we study and govern AI in science?

## Key idea

The impact of AI varies enormously by discipline, with data-rich fields (particle physics, genomics) differing fundamentally from interpretive humanities. Calls for ethnographic, differentiated analysis of AI's actual effects rather than utopian or dystopian generalization.

## Results

Main arguments:
- AI impact is discipline-specific: genomics, particle physics, materials science benefit most; humanities least
- Generic claims about "AI revolution in science" obscure this heterogeneity
- Need for empirical, ethnographic study of AI's actual effects in specific disciplinary contexts
- Warning against both uncritical adoption and blanket rejection

## Limitations

- Short opinion piece — not a systematic analysis
- Does not provide empirical data on the disciplinary differentiation it asserts

## Open questions

- What is the proper method for studying AI's discipline-specific effects?
- How should science policy differentiate AI support across disciplines?

## My take

A useful corrective that applies Knorr-Cetina's (1999) epistemic cultures insight to the AI debate: there is no "science" in general, only specific scientific practices in specific disciplinary contexts. Gao & Wang (2024) quantitative data supports the differentiation thesis. Schröter's call for ethnographic study is consistent with Knorr-Cetina's methodology.
