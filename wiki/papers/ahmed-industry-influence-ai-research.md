## Problem

AI research requires massive compute, large datasets, and skilled researchers — all increasingly controlled by industry. What are the implications of growing industrial dominance over the three key ingredients of modern AI research?

## Key idea

Industry is systematically dominating the three key ingredients of modern AI — computing, large datasets, and skilled researchers. This creates conflicts of interest, narrows research agendas toward commercially viable goals, and undermines the Mertonian norms of academic science (communalism, universalism, disinterestedness).

## Method

- Analysis of AI research trends using publication data and market analysis
- Published in *Science* 379(6635):884–886 (DOI 10.1126/science.ade2420)
- Authors: Nur Ahmed, Muntasir Wahed, Neil C. Thompson
- Focuses on structural shifts in who funds, conducts, and publishes AI research

## Results

- Industry-funded AI research has grown dramatically (5x in 10 years)
- Industry-led papers dominate the most influential AI benchmarks
- Academic AI researchers increasingly rely on industry compute and data
- Revolving door: leading academic AI researchers move to industry labs

## Limitations

- Science article is short (3 pages) — limited empirical detail
- Does not fully distinguish between beneficial industry-academia collaboration and problematic capture
- Primarily focused on computer science AI, not applied AI across domains

## Open questions

- How does industry influence affect the research questions that get asked in AI-for-science?
- Can open-source models (OLMo, etc.) counteract the concentration of AI resources?
- How should funders respond to industry dominance?

## My take

Concise but important diagnosis of a structural problem. Industrial dominance of AI violates Mertonian communalism (closed models) and disinterestedness (commercial objectives). The practical implication: published AI benchmark results should be treated with the same skepticism as industry-funded pharmaceutical research. Groeneveld et al. (2024) OLMo is partly a response to this problem.
