## Problem

Legal and social systems are characterized by hierarchical actors, exploitation dynamics, and emergent institutional evolution. Can LLM-based agent simulations reproduce known macro-level patterns (crime trends) while revealing micro-level emergent dynamics (cat-and-mouse behaviors, institutional corruption)?

## Key idea

Law in Silico constructs a hierarchical LLM agent framework grounded in real-world statistical distributions (agent demographics, behavioral patterns). The framework supports scenario-driven decision-making (single-shot and multi-turn) and dynamic legal systems where institutions can evolve, including modeling corruption. Simulation is validated against real-world crime data.

## Method

1. Initialize agent population with statistics-derived attributes (demographics, socioeconomic variables)
2. Implement hierarchical agent modeling: citizens, law enforcement, institutional actors
3. Simulate scenario-driven decision-making (single-shot and multi-turn interactions)
4. Model dynamic legal systems with institutional corruption possibility
5. Validate against real-world crime trend data

## Results

- Macro-level crime trends reproduced, aligned with real-world statistics
- Micro-level 'cat-and-mouse' behaviors emerge in exploitation scenarios
- Transparent, adaptive legal systems better protect vulnerable populations through emergent institutional evolution
- Corrupt systems show systematic exploitation patterns consistent with real-world dynamics

## Limitations

- Validation limited to crime trend alignment; individual agent behavior realism is unvalidated
- Legal system simplified compared to real-world institutional complexity
- Does not address long-term institutional stability or reform dynamics

## Open questions

- Can the framework simulate legislative reform and constitutional change?
- Does institutional corruption emergence depend on model capability or agent count?
- How do findings generalize across different legal systems/cultures?

## My take

A domain-specific application of generative agent modeling to legal systems. The macro-level validation against crime statistics is a useful grounding. The emergent micro-dynamics (cat-and-mouse behaviors) are interesting but require more rigorous validation. Contributes to the broader argument that LLM agent simulations can reproduce institutional dynamics beyond individual behavior.
