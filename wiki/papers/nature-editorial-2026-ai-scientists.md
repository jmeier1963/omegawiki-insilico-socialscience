## Problem

AI systems capable of automating hypothesis generation, experimental design, and results interpretation are entering scientific workflows. But institutions, funders, and publishers have not developed adequate governance frameworks for AI-assisted or AI-driven research.

## Key idea

An urgent call to action: the scientific community needs governance frameworks to handle research conducted or substantially assisted by AI agents, including norms for authorship, transparency, validation, and accountability. The editorial does not oppose AI in science but argues that governance has fallen behind capability.

## Method

- Nature editorial (unsigned; represents the journal's editorial position)
- Published: *Nature*, March 25, 2026 (DOI 10.1038/d41586-026-00934-w)

## Results

- AI systems are already automating significant portions of the research cycle
- Current governance frameworks (authorship, peer review, data sharing) were designed for human researchers
- Key governance gaps: who is responsible for AI-generated results? How should AI contributions be disclosed? How validated?
- Nature calls on institutions, funders, and publishers to develop explicit AI governance policies

## Limitations

- Editorial opinion — no original empirical research
- Specific governance recommendations are not detailed

## Open questions

- What specific governance frameworks would adequately address AI in science?
- How do DFG (2023/2025) and ICMJE (2023) policies compare to what is needed?

## My take

Important as a signal that the world's most prestigious scientific journal recognizes a governance crisis. The editorial reflects the accumulated evidence of papers like Liang et al. (2024), Messeri & Crockett (2024), and the policy responses (DFG, ICMJE) as insufficient. The March 2026 date makes this the most recent statement in the governance debate.
