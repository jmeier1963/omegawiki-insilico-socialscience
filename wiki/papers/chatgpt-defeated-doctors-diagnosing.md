## Problem

Can AI chatbots outperform human physicians at diagnostic reasoning, and do they improve physician performance when used as decision aids?

## Key idea

A controlled study (JAMA Network Open, 2024) found ChatGPT-4 alone scored 90% on complex diagnostic cases vs. 76% for physicians with ChatGPT and 74% for physicians without. Physicians did not significantly benefit from access to AI — partly because "humans can be stubborn when AI tells them things they don't agree with."

## Method

- Study by Dr. Adam Rodman (Boston internal medicine) and colleagues
- 50 licensed physicians, complex case histories
- Three arms: ChatGPT alone, physicians with ChatGPT, physicians without ChatGPT
- Cases based on actual unpublished patients (no training data leakage)
- Published: JAMA Network Open, November 2024

## Results

- ChatGPT alone: 90% diagnostic accuracy
- Physicians + ChatGPT: 76%
- Physicians alone: 74%
- Physicians failed to fully benefit from AI: resistance to AI-suggested diagnoses when they disagreed

## Limitations

- Small study (50 physicians)
- Complex diagnostic cases may not represent typical clinical practice
- "Accuracy" defined by case report rubric — may not match real-world patient outcomes
- Physicians' AI skill level varied; experienced AI users might perform better

## Open questions

- What physician training protocols best enable AI-augmented diagnostic performance?
- Does AI diagnostic superiority extend beyond complex case studies to routine clinical practice?

## My take

The finding that physician+AI performs worse than AI alone is counterintuitive and important: it suggests that human-AI teaming requires specific skill sets that most physicians don't yet have. Relevant to broader questions about human-AI collaboration in any expert domain including social science research.
