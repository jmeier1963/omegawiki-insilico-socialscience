## Problem

Autonomous learning machines (neural networks, genetic algorithms) can develop behavior that their designers and operators did not intend and cannot predict. Who bears moral and legal responsibility for the outcomes? Can traditional responsibility frameworks handle this?

## Key idea

Autonomous learning machines create a "responsibility gap": because their future behavior is in principle unpredictable by designers or operators, neither can be held fully morally responsible for harmful outcomes. Traditional frameworks that require knowledge, control, and intent cannot close this gap.

## Method

- Philosophical analysis of moral responsibility conditions
- Published: *Ethics and Information Technology* 6(3):175–183 (DOI 10.1007/s10676-004-3422-1)
- Andreas Matthias (first significant academic treatment of AI responsibility gap)

## Results

- Traditional responsibility ascription requires: knowledge of likely outcomes + control over actions + agency
- Learning machines violate the control condition: their learned behavior may diverge from designer intent
- The gap is structural: no individual (designer, operator, user) fully satisfies the responsibility conditions
- Proposed solutions (strict liability, licensing) are discussed but not fully resolved

## Limitations

- Written before modern deep learning — the argument is more acute with contemporary systems
- The "in principle unpredictability" claim may be too strong for all cases
- Does not distinguish between types of autonomy or degrees of learning

## Open questions

- How does the responsibility gap apply to AI systems deployed in scientific research?
- Does ICMJE/DFG authorship policy partially address the responsibility gap by requiring human accountability?
- Can AI agents be treated as "moral proxies" rather than agents?

## My take

The founding text of the AI responsibility gap debate. Prescient — written before modern deep learning but directly applicable to it. The gap has only grown wider with LLMs that can generate unpredictable outputs. Together with Foucault (1969) on the author function, provides conceptual vocabulary for the AI authorship and accountability debates in science.
