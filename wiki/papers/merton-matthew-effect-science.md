## Problem

The sociology of science notes that credit and recognition in science are unevenly distributed. Why do eminent scientists receive disproportionately greater credit for collaborative or simultaneously discovered findings than their less-known counterparts?

## Key idea

The Matthew Effect in science: "unto every one that hath shall be given" — eminent scientists receive more credit for the same contributions than unknown scientists. This cumulative advantage systematically shapes the reward and recognition structure of science, amplifying existing status differences.

## Method

- Empirical case studies of priority disputes, collaborative publications, and multiple discoveries
- Interviews with Nobel laureates and their collaborators
- Published in *Science* 159(3810):56–63 (DOI 10.1126/science.159.3810.56)

## Results

- In collaborative papers, the most famous co-author receives disproportionate citation credit
- Multiple simultaneous discoveries: the famous scientist's version becomes the "official" one
- Reviewers and grant committees are biased toward known names even for identical work
- The effect compounds over time: early advantage → more resources → more publications → more fame

## Limitations

- Primarily anecdotal; later research has confirmed the effect quantitatively (citation analysis)
- Focuses on extreme cases (Nobel laureates) which may not represent typical science
- Does not propose corrective mechanisms

## Open questions

- Does AI amplify or attenuate the Matthew Effect? (AI may give resources to all, but also amplify the reach of already-famous researchers)
- Gao & Wang (2024) find that AI adoption is concentrated at elite institutions — consistent with Matthew Effect dynamics

## My take

A classic and much-cited paper that named and documented a fundamental feature of scientific stratification. Directly relevant to debates about AI and scientific inequality: if AI tools are first adopted by already-advantaged researchers, the Matthew Effect predicts the gap will widen. The Leiden Manifesto (Hicks 2015) is partly a response to Matthew Effect dynamics in bibliometrics.
