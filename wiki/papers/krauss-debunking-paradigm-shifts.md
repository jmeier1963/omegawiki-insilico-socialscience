## Problem

Kuhn's paradigm-shift model of scientific change is widely cited and influential. But is it empirically correct? How often do major scientific advances actually involve displacement (Kuhnian revolution) vs. cumulative addition (normal science)?

## Key idea

Systematic analysis of over 750 major scientific discoveries (Nobel and non-Nobel) finds that fewer than 1% have been abandoned — directly challenging Kuhn's paradigm-shift model and arguing that revolutionary rhetoric overstates discontinuity. Scientific progress is overwhelmingly cumulative.

## Method

- Systematic analysis of 750+ major scientific discoveries across multiple disciplines
- Coded each discovery as: abandoned (Kuhnian revolution) vs. retained (cumulative progress)
- Published: *Proceedings of the Royal Society A* 480(2302), article 20240141 (DOI 10.1098/rspa.2024.0141)

## Results

- <1% of major discoveries have been abandoned by the subsequent scientific community
- The overwhelmingly dominant mode of scientific progress is cumulative, not revolutionary
- Even major conceptual shifts (evolution, quantum mechanics) retained and extended prior knowledge
- The Kuhnian paradigm-shift model overstates discontinuity and is empirically false as a general model

## Limitations

- Selection of "major discoveries" involves judgment calls
- Measuring "abandonment" is not straightforward — some ideas are transformed, not abandoned
- Doesn't address the role of paradigm shifts in specific subfields or periods

## Open questions

- Does AI-driven science follow the same cumulative pattern, or does it introduce genuine discontinuities?
- Are there historical periods where paradigm shifts were more common than Krauss finds on average?

## My take

An important empirical challenge to one of the most cited frameworks in science studies. Kuhn is often invoked to frame AI as a "paradigm shift" in science; Krauss suggests this rhetoric overstates the discontinuity. The cumulative finding is more consistent with Laudan's (1977) problem-solving model than with Kuhn. Worth pairing with Masterman (1970) on the ambiguity of "paradigm."
