## Problem

Deep learning models are opaque — we cannot track why they produce their outputs. Philosophical pessimism treats this as an epistemic failure that limits AI's role in genuine scientific discovery. Is this pessimism warranted?

## Key idea

The discovery/justification distinction dissolves the apparent contradiction between opaque models and legitimate scientific knowledge. Opacity in the context of *discovery* is not an epistemic failure — models function as detection instruments whose outputs trigger traditional justificatory practices (replication, mechanistic modeling, experiment). The epistemic problem is in justification, not discovery.

## Method

- Philosophical analysis drawing on philosophy of science (discovery/justification distinction; instruments)
- Case studies from scientific uses of deep learning
- Published: *Philosophy of Science* 90(5):1089–1099 (DOI 10.1017/psa.2023.8)
- Eamon Duede (University of Chicago)

## Results

- Distinction between context of discovery and context of justification: opacity is only problematic in justification
- Deep learning models function like scientific instruments (telescopes, microscopes) — their outputs are treated as evidence, not explanations
- Opacity-as-problem misapplies justificatory standards to discovery
- The right response to opaque AI results is triggering additional justificatory research, not rejecting the discovery

## Limitations

- The discovery/justification distinction is itself contested (it was largely abandoned in the late 20th century)
- May underestimate cases where opacity in the model prevents useful justification from being triggered
- Does not address cases where the opaque model's output is used directly (no additional justification)

## Open questions

- What are the conditions under which opacity in discovery is acceptable vs. unacceptable?
- Does the instrument analogy hold for models that generate (not just detect) outputs?

## My take

An important philosophical reframing. The instrument analogy is the key move: we don't require scientists to understand the quantum mechanics of their spectrometer to trust its readings, provided the spectrometer is validated. Alvarado (2022) takes a different view (opacity as agent-independent and structural). Sullivan (2022) focuses on linking assumptions rather than opacity per se. Worth reading all three together.
