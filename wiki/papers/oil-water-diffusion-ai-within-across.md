## Problem

Popular narratives claim AI is "transforming" scientific fields. But is AI research actually integrating with traditional research in those fields, or merely appearing alongside it?

## Key idea

Analyzing ~80 million publications across 20 fields (1985–2022), AI-engaged research grew 13x across all disciplines. But document embedding analysis reveals an "oil-and-water phenomenon": AI-engaged papers spread across fields but remain semantically separate from non-AI-engaged papers in the same venues, failing to integrate.

## Method

- ~80 million publications across 20 scientific fields (1985–2022)
- Document embeddings to measure semantic similarity between AI-engaged and non-AI research
- Analysis of venue broadening: did AI-engaged work diversify within field publication venues?
- Longitudinal design: tracks diffusion over 37 years

## Results

- 13x growth in AI-engaged publications across all fields
- AI engagement widened within disciplinary venues (more diverse publication venues)
- Semantic tension: AI and non-AI papers in the same field are systematically semantically distant
- "Oil-and-water phenomenon" — ubiquity without integration

## Limitations

- Study ends in 2022; post-ChatGPT era may show different integration patterns
- Semantic distance via embeddings doesn't distinguish productive cross-pollination from mere co-location
- "AI-engaged" definition may vary in sensitivity across different fields

## Open questions

- Has the oil-and-water gap narrowed since 2022 as LLMs made AI more accessible?
- What interventions (training, incentives, journals) might bridge the semantic gap?

## My take

The oil-and-water finding is counterintuitive and important. The surface narrative of "AI integrating into every field" is empirically questionable. This matters for science policy: funding cross-disciplinary AI integration may be harder than expected if the communities are fundamentally operating in different epistemic registers.
