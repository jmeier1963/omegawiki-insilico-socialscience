## Problem

Are computer simulations a genuinely new class of scientific methods, or are they reducible to existing methods (analytical calculation, experiment)? If they are genuinely new, what makes them epistemically novel?

## Key idea

Computer simulation methods constitute a genuinely new class of scientific methods because they introduce features that have no analogue in classical science: (1) computational tractability as an epistemic constraint (some calculations are only feasible computationally); (2) a new kind of epistemic opacity from non-human operations that cannot be followed by any human mind.

## Method

- Philosophical analysis
- Published: *Synthese* 169(3):615–626 (DOI 10.1007/s11229-008-9435-2)
- Paul Humphreys (University of Virginia)
- Companion to *Extending Ourselves* (2004)

## Results

- Three novel features of computer simulation: (1) template-instantiation structure, (2) computational tractability constraint, (3) human-inaccessible operations
- These features cannot be captured by the traditional categories of model, experiment, or calculation
- The opacity arising from non-human operations is a principled feature, not a practical limitation to be overcome by more computing power

## Limitations

- The "novelty" claim is contested — some argue simulations are just complex calculations
- The template-instantiation framework needs more development
- Pre-dates deep learning; some arguments need updating for neural networks

## Open questions

- Does deep learning introduce additional novelty beyond what Humphreys identified for simulations?
- Is AI-driven scientific discovery continuous with simulation-based discovery or genuinely distinct?

## My take

The foundational paper for treating computer simulation as methodologically novel. Humphreys' insight that computational tractability is a new kind of epistemic constraint — not just speed — is the key contribution. This sets up Beisbart (2021) and Boge (2022) who extend the analysis to deep learning.
