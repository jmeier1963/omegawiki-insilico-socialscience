## Problem

AI is already being applied to scientific problems, but what are the bottlenecks to accelerating scientific discovery with AI, and what research agenda would actually close them?

## Key idea

A high-level agenda paper from Neil Lawrence and Jessica Montgomery (Cambridge) identifying key challenges in deploying AI for scientific discovery: data quality, model interpretability, experimental integration, and incentive alignment. Argues for a coordinated programme rather than ad-hoc application.

## Method

- Agenda/perspective paper (not primary research)
- Published: *Royal Society Open Science* (DOI 10.1098/rsos.231130), 2024
- Authors: Neil D. Lawrence (Cambridge, DeepMind Visiting Fellow), Jessica Montgomery (Cambridge)

## Results

Key arguments:
- Bottlenecks in AI-for-science are not primarily model capabilities but data infrastructure, experimental feedback loops, and reproducibility
- Interpretability matters for scientific use: black-box prediction is less useful than explainable models
- Need for AI systems that can close the hypothesis-experiment loop, not just predict from existing data
- Institutional incentives misaligned: reward structures favor publications over reusable tools

## Limitations

- Agenda paper — does not provide empirical evidence for claims
- Primarily reflects a UK/Cambridge perspective
- Does not address differences across scientific disciplines

## Open questions

- Which bottlenecks (data, interpretability, loop-closing) are most binding in practice?
- How should research funders evaluate AI-for-science infrastructure vs. individual model papers?

## My take

A useful high-level framing from credible authors (Lawrence is one of the UK's most prominent ML scientists). More policy-oriented than technically specific. Best read alongside Gao & Wang (2024) for empirical data on where AI is already succeeding, and Ahmed et al. (2023) for the industry-incentive problem.
