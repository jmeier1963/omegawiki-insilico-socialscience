## Problem

Generative AI has prompted "substitution proposals" — replacing human research participants with AI surrogates in HCI, psychology, and social science. What are the arguments for and against such substitution, and do the stated goals hold up?

## Key idea

LLM substitution proposals promise cost reduction and diversity gains but conflict with foundational values of human participation: representation, inclusion, and genuine understanding of human experience. AI surrogates create an "illusion" of inclusion that masks exclusion.

## Method

Scoping review of substitution proposals in HCI, psychology, and user research. Analyzes the stated motivations and implicit assumptions of proposals to replace human participants with generative AI models.

## Results

- Proposals are motivated by (1) cost reduction, (2) diversity/scalability, (3) hard-to-recruit population access
- However, these goals conflict with: genuine representation (models encode existing biases), inclusion (marginalized voices replaced by majority-weighted models), understanding (AI responses are not lived experience)
- The "diversity" gain is illusory — models reflect training data demographics, not the populations ostensibly simulated
- Highlights risks specific to inclusion-focused research contexts

## Limitations

- Scoping review, not systematic — no quantitative meta-analysis
- Focused on HCI/psychology context, may not generalize to all social science domains
- Positions are argued, not empirically tested in this paper

## Open questions

- Can participatory design methods be integrated with AI-assisted research without substituting human voice?
- How should IRBs evaluate substitution proposals?
- Does the illusion problem apply equally to well-defined survey tasks vs. open-ended qualitative research?

## My take

Important ethical perspective that complements empirical findings: even when LLMs numerically replicate aggregate distributions, they substitute the *experience* of research participation (representation in process) not just the data. Relevant critique of optimistic silicon sampling claims, though the "illusion" framing may overstate the case for narrow, well-scoped research questions.
