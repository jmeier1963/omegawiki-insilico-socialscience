## Problem

AI tools are rapidly adopted in the natural sciences. Does this adoption benefit individual scientists, the scientific community, or both — and are there trade-offs? Hao et al. (2025, Nature) examined 41M+ papers (1980–2025) and identified a paradox that Storey contextualises here.

## Key idea

AI adoption is a double-edged sword: it amplifies individual scientists' output and career trajectories but simultaneously contracts the collective diversity of research domains explored. Scientists using AI publish more, get cited more, and advance faster — but AI steers the community toward data-rich, already-established fields rather than into new ones.

## Method

Commentary (News & Views) synthesising Hao et al. (2025): analysis of ~41M natural science papers published 1980–2025, ~311,000 AI-augmented. Covers three AI eras: machine learning, deep learning, generative AI. Key metrics: citation counts, journal impact, career progression speed, breadth of domains explored per researcher over time.

## Results

- AI-augmented researchers in all three eras are more highly cited and published in higher-impact journals than non-AI peers.
- Scientists adopting AI publish more papers and establish careers faster.
- AI adoption is associated with **contraction** in the range of scientific fields explored: more papers, fewer domains.
- AI automates established (data-rich) domains rather than enabling exploration of new ones.

## Limitations

- Commentary, not primary research — evidence is interpreted from Hao et al.
- Causal inference is challenging: selection effects (more productive scientists may both adopt AI and focus on established domains) are not fully ruled out.
- Generative AI era (post-2020) is at the edge of the dataset and may not be fully captured.

## Open questions

- Does the domain-narrowing effect persist as generative AI matures?
- Is narrowing harmful if narrow-but-deep AI-enabled research produces more breakthroughs?
- How does the finding generalise to social sciences, humanities, and AI research itself?
- Could policy interventions (cross-disciplinary AI grants) reverse the narrowing?

## My take

The productivity-diversity paradox is one of the most important empirical findings about AI's systemic effects on science. The commentary frames it well but doesn't add new analysis. The original Hao et al. paper (not yet ingested) should be prioritised for full ingest. This finding complements the Oil & Water gap ([[ai-science-adoption-gap]]): together they paint a picture of AI as an amplifier-but-segregator, accelerating individual careers while homogenising the research landscape.
