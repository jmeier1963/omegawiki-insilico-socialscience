## Problem

"Opacity" in computer simulations is often discussed in binary terms: either a simulation is opaque or it is not. But opacity is more complex. What is a precise, graded account of simulation opacity and its epistemic implications?

## Key idea

Computer simulations are opaque to the degree that it is difficult to know and understand *why* their outcomes arise. The paper proposes a graded, process-oriented explication of opacity that moves beyond simple transparent/opaque dichotomies, situating simulation opacity within the broader epistemology of science.

## Method

- Philosophical analysis; close engagement with philosophy of simulation literature
- Published: *Synthese* 199(3–4):11643–11666 (DOI 10.1007/s11229-021-03305-2)
- Claus Beisbart (University of Bern)

## Results

- Opacity is a relational and graded concept: a simulation is opaque *for a given agent*, *at a given level of description*, *to a given degree*
- Process-oriented opacity: what matters is whether the causal-computational process is followable
- Six dimensions of opacity: computational, parameter, algorithmic, emergent, model, and conceptual opacity
- High opacity in some dimension need not preclude scientific understanding in others

## Limitations

- Highly technical philosophical text; limited empirical grounding
- The six-dimension taxonomy may be over-detailed for practical use
- Does not engage with the specific opacity properties of deep learning

## Open questions

- How does the graded opacity framework apply to foundation models vs. simulation models?
- Can the six-dimension taxonomy guide practical opacity assessment in AI-science pipelines?

## My take

The most careful philosophical analysis of simulation opacity, but less focused on ML than Boge (2022) or Duede (2023). Useful for precision in arguments about specific types of opacity. The graded, process-oriented approach is the right conceptual move — avoids the binary "it's a black box" dismissal.
