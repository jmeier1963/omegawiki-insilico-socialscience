## Problem

Humans routinely use physical actions and external tools to reduce internal cognitive demand. What are the mechanisms and cognitive consequences of this "cognitive offloading," and when is it adaptive vs. maladaptive?

## Key idea

Cognitive offloading — using physical actions or external tools to reduce internal cognitive demand — is a pervasive and adaptive feature of human cognition. The review maps the types of offloading, the mechanisms that trigger it, and its cognitive consequences, providing a theoretical framework applicable to AI-assisted thinking.

## Method

- Theoretical review of experimental cognitive science literature
- Published: *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* 20(9):676–688 (DOI 10.1016/j.tics.2016.07.002)
- Evan F. Risko (Waterloo), Sam J. Gilbert (UCL)

## Results

Types of cognitive offloading:
- **Spatial**: using physical arrangements to reduce computation (sorting cards, using fingers)
- **Temporal**: using reminders/notes to reduce prospective memory demand
- **Biological**: using the body to offload (gesturing while thinking)

Mechanisms: triggered by cognitive demand + available offloading medium
Consequences: reduces errors, allows more cognitive resources for other tasks — adaptive in most conditions

## Limitations

- Focus on traditional offloading (writing, physical arrangement); predates AI tools
- The "adaptive" framing may underweight cases where offloading prevents skill acquisition
- Does not address the learning consequences of offloading

## Open questions

- Does cognitive offloading to AI tools differ mechanistically from offloading to notebooks or calculators?
- What are the conditions under which AI cognitive offloading becomes maladaptive (Bastani 2024, Kosmyna 2025)?

## My take

The theoretical grounding for debates about AI cognitive offloading. Risko & Gilbert treat offloading as generally adaptive — a contrast with Carr (2010) and Bastani (2024) who identify maladaptive cases. The framework helps clarify when offloading is good (freeing cognitive resources for harder tasks) vs. bad (preventing skill acquisition). The AI case may be a qualitatively different regime.
