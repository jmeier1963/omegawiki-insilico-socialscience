## Problem

Algorithms are increasingly embedded in culture, shaping what we consume, how we move, and what we value — yet their inner workings remain opaque. What is the cultural imagination surrounding algorithms, and how do idealized computational abstractions collide with messy reality?

## Key idea

Algorithms have roots not just in mathematical logic but also in cybernetics, philosophy, and "magical thinking." To understand the gap between algorithmic abstraction and messy human reality, we need **algorithmic reading** — a humanities scholarship that attends to process. The algorithm is a "culture machine" that encodes human values while appearing to transcend them.

## Method

- Book by Ed Finn (Arizona State University, Center for Science and the Imagination)
- MIT Press, 2017; 272 pages
- Case studies: Siri, Netflix, Cow Clicker, Bitcoin, Google, Uber, Facebook
- Methodological argument for "experimental humanities"

## Results

- Algorithms deploy idealized concepts (rationality, optimization) in messy social reality — with unpredictable results
- Examples: Google's anticipatory questioning, Uber's black-box accounting, Netflix's aesthetic algorithms
- "Algorithmic reading" as critical methodology for understanding platform power
- Algorithms encode and reproduce the values of their makers while claiming neutrality

## Limitations

- Published 2017 — pre-LLM; the cultural imagination around GPT-era AI is not addressed
- Humanities framing — proposing "algorithmic reading" but not empirically testing it
- Case studies are selective; systematic analysis is missing

## Open questions

- How does the "culture machine" framework extend to LLMs that generate language, not just organize it?
- What are the ideological commitments encoded in LLM training decisions?

## My take

Foundational cultural theory of algorithms, predating the LLM era but more relevant than ever. The "culture machine" framing is exactly the right lens for understanding why LLMs reflect specific cultural viewpoints (cf. [[whose-opinions-language-models-reflect]]). Useful as theoretical background for in-silico social science methodology.
