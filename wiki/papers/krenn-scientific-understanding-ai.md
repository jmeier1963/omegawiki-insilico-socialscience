## Problem

AI tools produce impressive predictive performance in scientific domains (materials, drug discovery, physics). But prediction is not understanding. What forms of scientific understanding can AI actually contribute?

## Key idea

The paper distinguishes three modes of AI-assisted scientific understanding: (1) making predictions, (2) generating hypotheses, (3) building conceptual frameworks. AI can contribute genuine understanding (not just prediction) when its outputs are interpretable in terms that humans can assimilate into existing scientific knowledge.

## Method

- Conceptual/theoretical analysis with illustrative case studies
- Authors: Mario Krenn, Robert Pollice, Si Yue Guo, et al. + Alan Aspuru-Guzik (12 authors)
- Published in *Nature Reviews Physics* 4:761–769 (DOI 10.1038/s42254-022-00518-3)
- Case studies from quantum optics, drug discovery, materials science

## Results

Three levels of understanding:
- **Prediction**: AI produces accurate forecasts; understanding is shallow (AlphaFold initial use)
- **Hypothesis generation**: AI points to unexpected regularities that humans can test and interpret
- **Conceptual framework building**: AI helps construct new categories and relations (rare but possible)

Key examples: REINVENT (drug design), neural net discovers group theory (Socrates system), SELFIES molecular language for AI design

## Limitations

- "Understanding" is used in multiple senses without fully engaging de Regt (2017)
- The three-level framework is intuitive but not rigorously formalized
- Does not address the monoculture risk raised by Messeri & Crockett (2024)

## Open questions

- Under what conditions do AI-generated conceptual frameworks become widely adopted by the scientific community?
- Is there a fourth mode of understanding: AI discovering mechanisms rather than patterns?

## My take

The most philosophically careful treatment of AI and scientific understanding by scientists (rather than philosophers). The three-level framework is a useful heuristic. The key insight — that interpretability in human terms is required for genuine understanding — is consistent with de Regt (2017) but arrives from the scientific rather than philosophical side.
