## Problem

How should researchers and institutions reset their expectations about AI tools like GPT-4, given that they violate conventional assumptions about how software should work?

## Key idea

AI tools like GPT-4 fundamentally challenge the assumption that technology requires precise inputs and predictable outputs. They excel with "vague prompts and messy data" and function as collaborative partners rather than deterministic tools. Institutions — especially academia — must rethink practices around publishing, education, and professional standards.

## Method

- Essay by Terence Tao, Professor of Mathematics, UCLA
- Published June 12, 2023 in Microsoft Unlocked AI Anthology
- Personal reflection from a world-leading mathematician's perspective

## Results

- GPT-4 as "compassionate listener, enthusiastic sounding board, creative muse, translator or teacher, or devil's advocate"
- Traditional tool assumptions (precise input → precise output) no longer apply
- AI will enable faster research and learning but require institutional adaptation
- Expect "shocking demonstrations" alongside "embarrassing mistakes" — both will persist

## Limitations

- Personal/anecdotal reflection, not empirical study
- Published early (June 2023) relative to current AI capability levels
- Institutional recommendations are broad rather than specific

## Open questions

- Which institutional norms in academia can adapt quickly vs. require fundamental restructuring?
- How does AI assistance change the skill distribution required for cutting-edge mathematics?

## My take

A high-signal perspective precisely because it comes from Tao — one of the greatest living mathematicians. His emphasis on "vague prompts and messy data" as AI's actual working domain is counterintuitive and practically important. Pairs well with his later work on the Erdős problems ([[semi-autonomous-mathematics-discovery-gemini-case]]) as an example of the collaboration he anticipated.
