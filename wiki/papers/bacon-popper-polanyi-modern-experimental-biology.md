## Problem

How have the epistemologies of Bacon (induction), Popper (hypothesis/falsification), and Polanyi (tacit knowledge, scientific authority) each shaped modern experimental biology — and how does big data genomics change this balance?

## Key idea

Induction, scientific authority, and critical hypothesis testing have all been integral to biology's history since the 19th century. Big data genomics represents a shift: induction plays a *major* role while Polanyi's epistemic apprenticeship and Popper's hypothesis-test cycle are *marginalized*. This epistemological shift has consequences for how scientific knowledge is validated.

## Method

- Essay by Ute Deichmann (Ben-Gurion University of the Negev)
- Published in Springer volume honoring Joseph Agassi, 2017
- DOI: 10.1007/978-3-319-57669-5_12
- Cases from genetics, embryology, molecular biology, genomics

## Results

- Bacon's induction: always present in biology, now dominant in big data era
- Popper's hypothesis-testing: central to classical experimental biology, now marginalized in genomics
- Polanyi's tacit knowledge and apprenticeship: key to historical scientific training, at risk from algorithmic approaches
- Big data genomics is closer to Baconian induction than to Popperian hypothesis testing

## Limitations

- Written before LLMs; the emergence of AI as experimental partner adds another epistemological layer
- Focus on biology; may not generalize to physics, economics, social science

## Open questions

- Does AI-generated hypothesis formation reinstate Popperian testing at scale, or further entrench induction?
- What is the epistemological status of in-silico experiments — are they closer to Bacon or Popper?

## My take
