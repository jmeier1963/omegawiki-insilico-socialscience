## Problem

AI algorithms are increasingly applied to pure mathematics and theoretical physics, but the field lacks a unified conceptual framework for understanding how AI contributes to theoretical discovery.

## Key idea

Three paradigms structure AI-driven mathematical discovery: **top-down** (theory → application, classical deductive reasoning), **bottom-up** (pattern-finding in data → conjecture), and **meta-mathematics** (AI reasoning about mathematics itself). Human-AI collaboration — not AI replacement — is the viable future for theoretical discovery.

## Method

Conceptual review paper by Yang-Hui He (City, University of London / Oxford). Synthesizes examples from algebraic geometry, string theory, and number theory. Examines historical analogues and recent AI applications.

## Results

- Three-paradigm taxonomy provides coherent framework for AI's role in theoretical science
- Bottom-up AI (pattern recognition, conjecture generation) is most tractable with current tools
- Top-down AI (theorem proving, deductive reasoning) advancing via formal verification tools
- Meta-mathematics (AI reasoning about proof systems) is most speculative
- Main conclusion: hybrid human-AI collaboration will become integral; human theorists remain essential

## Limitations

- Review paper based on existing examples; no new empirical results
- Taxonomy is descriptive, not predictive
- Author's expertise is primarily in mathematical physics; computer science perspective is limited

## Open questions

- Can bottom-up AI generate conjectures that lead to genuinely new mathematical theories?
- What does it mean for AI to contribute to pure mathematics beyond verification?

## My take

A thoughtful taxonomy from a working mathematical physicist. The three-paradigm framework is genuinely useful for positioning different AI approaches. The conclusion (hybrid collaboration, not replacement) reflects the current reality accurately. The paper's value is in providing structure, not in empirical novelty.
