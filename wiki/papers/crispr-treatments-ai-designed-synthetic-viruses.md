## Problem

The convergence of AI and synthetic biology enables design of novel viral genomes at scale, outpacing conventional biosecurity screening. What programmable countermeasures can be rapidly deployed against AI-designed or engineered viruses?

## Key idea

CRISPR antivirals (Cas9 for DNA viruses, Cas13 for RNA viruses) are **sequence-reprogrammable** and can be redesigned as fast as a new viral sequence is characterized — making them the most promising countermeasure paradigm against AI-designed synthetic threats. The speed of CRISPR reprogramming matches the speed of threat emergence.

## Method

- Review paper by Douglas P. Gladue and Alison O'Mahony (Seek Labs)
- Covers Cas9 for DNA virus targets (Orthopoxviruses, HSV-1, ASFV)
- Covers Cas13 for RNA virus targets (Influenza A, Dengue, RSV)
- Analyzes AI-synthetic biology convergence as dual-use risk

## Results

- Cas9 degrades DNA viral genomes; Cas13 targets RNA viral genomes
- CRISPR reprogramming can be faster than conventional antiviral development
- AI lowers barriers to novel viral genome design, including novel proteins that evade conventional screening
- Programmable CRISPR countermeasures address the "AI-enabled proliferation" threat specifically

## Limitations

- Review paper: no new experimental results
- CRISPR antiviral delivery remains a significant practical challenge
- AI-designed threat model may be speculative relative to natural pandemic risk
- Regulatory pathway for rapid CRISPR antiviral deployment is unclear

## Open questions

- Can CRISPR antiviral deployment timelines be compressed to match pandemic-speed threats?
- How should AI-designed biological threat screening be integrated into current biosecurity infrastructure?
