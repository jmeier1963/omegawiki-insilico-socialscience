## Problem

Peer review is the cornerstone of scientific quality control. But is it effective? What does the empirical evidence say about whether peer review reliably identifies important research, detects errors, and prevents fraud?

## Key idea

Peer review is slow, expensive, ineffective at detecting fraud and error, prone to bias, and lacks empirical evidence of reliability. The scientific community's near-religious faith in peer review is not empirically warranted; radical alternatives should be explored.

## Method

- Evidence review drawing on 13 years as BMJ editor + published research on peer review
- Published: *JRSM* 99(4):178–182 (DOI 10.1177/014107680609900414)
- Richard Smith (former BMJ editor)

## Results

- Peer review is poor at detecting fraud: most famous frauds passed peer review
- Biased: women, unknown authors, certain nationalities receive worse reviews
- Slow: average 6 months from submission to acceptance
- Inconsistent: reviewers frequently disagree; inter-rater reliability is low
- No evidence that it improves the papers it accepts vs. rejects

## Limitations

- Evidence from biomedical publishing — may not generalize to all fields
- The alternatives (open review, post-publication review) also have problems
- Reflects the state of peer review in 2006 — some structural changes have occurred since

## Open questions

- Does AI peer review (DFG 2025) make peer review worse (AI-modified content) or better (more consistent)?
- What level of AI assistance in peer review is acceptable?
- Would AI reviewers have better inter-rater reliability than human reviewers?

## My take

The most clearly argued case that peer review's reputation far exceeds its evidence base. Relevant to Liang et al. (2024) on AI-modified peer reviews — the system was already imperfect before AI assistance. DFG (2025) allowing AI in review should be read in context: AI is entering a system that already had reliability problems. The question is whether it makes things better or worse.
