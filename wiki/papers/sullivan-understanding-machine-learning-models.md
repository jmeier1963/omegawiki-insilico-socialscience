## Problem

Machine learning models are often opaque. Does their opacity limit the scientific understanding they can provide? When, if ever, can opaque ML models contribute to genuine scientific understanding?

## Key idea

Opacity or complexity of ML models is not itself what limits scientific understanding. What matters is whether scientists have well-established "linking assumptions" — a chain of theoretical and empirical connections linking the model's inputs and outputs to the real-world target phenomena. Without these, even a transparent model cannot provide understanding.

## Method

- Philosophical analysis drawing on de Regt (2017), Hempel, and philosophy of simulation
- Case study: ML models for poverty prediction from satellite imagery
- Published: *British Journal for the Philosophy of Science* 73(1):109–133 (DOI 10.1093/bjps/axz035)
- Emily Sullivan (TU Eindhoven)

## Results

- "Link uncertainty" (lack of linking assumptions) is the main obstacle to understanding, not opacity per se
- A transparent model with no linking assumptions provides less understanding than an opaque model with strong linking assumptions
- AlphaFold example: opacity is not the key issue; the linking assumptions (protein structure → function) are well-established
- Implications for which ML applications can/cannot provide understanding

## Limitations

- "Linking assumptions" is somewhat vague — it requires further operationalization
- Focuses on understanding from models used in explanatory contexts; may not cover all scientific uses
- The poverty-from-satellite-imagery case is contested as a clear example

## Open questions

- How do we establish linking assumptions for AI in new domains (drug discovery, materials)?
- Does Sullivan's framework imply AlphaFold provides understanding once linking assumptions are established?

## My take

The most influential recent philosophical treatment of AI and scientific understanding. The linking-assumptions framing reframes the debate: instead of asking "is the model transparent?" we should ask "are there established connections between model inputs/outputs and the phenomena we want to understand?" Directly challenges Messeri & Crockett (2024) who treat opacity as the primary risk.
