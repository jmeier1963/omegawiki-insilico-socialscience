## Problem

AI scaling debates focus on model capabilities, but intelligence may not be primarily a property of individual models. This position paper argues that AI intelligence emerges from socially structured, distributed systems — and that future AI governance and design must reflect this.

## Key idea

AI intelligence arises from **societies of thought** — multi-perspective deliberation systems with conflict, role-based interaction, and institutional norms. Drawing on cognitive science, evolutionary theory, and social organization, future AI should be designed with:
- Institutional norms and hierarchy
- Collective dynamics akin to human societies
- Hybrid human-AI "centaur" systems
- Recursive self-organizing agent ensembles

This shifts AI scaling from raw compute to **social architecture**.

## Method

Position/conceptual paper. Synthesizes cognitive science, evolutionary theory, and social organization theory to argue for social architecture as the key design principle for advanced AI. Critiques individual reinforcement learning as insufficient for governance and high-stakes decision-making.

## Results

Main arguments:
- Intelligence is a collective, emergent property of socially structured systems, not a property of individual models
- Human cognition itself is shaped by social structures (distributed cognition, extended mind theory)
- Future AI must be designed with institutional alignment, not just individual RL alignment
- Centaur systems (hybrid human-AI) operating through recursive agent ensembles are the near-term trajectory

## Limitations

- Position paper without empirical support
- "Societies of thought" argument is analogical rather than mechanistic
- Institutional design for AI is underspecified

## Open questions

- What institutional structures are most effective for AI collective deliberation?
- How can institutional alignment be operationalized in practice?
- Does collective AI intelligence exhibit emergent capabilities absent from individual models?

## My take

A thought-provoking position paper connecting AI design to social theory. The societies-of-thought concept connects to the empirical findings in 2601.10825 (reasoning models). The governance implications are interesting but underspecified. More useful as a conceptual framing than as a concrete research program.
