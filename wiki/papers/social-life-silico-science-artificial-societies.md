## Problem

How can computational modeling of interacting artificial agents illuminate social phenomena that are difficult to study empirically? What is the epistemological status of simulation as a scientific method?

## Key idea

Artificial societies — computational models of interacting heterogeneous agents — can "grow" social phenomena from the bottom up, providing a middle path between formal mathematical theory and empirical observation. Simulation is a third way of doing science.

## Method

- Chapter in SAGE Handbook reviewing the history and methodology of artificial society modeling
- Discusses seminal models: Schelling's segregation, Axelrod's cooperation, Epstein & Axtell's Sugarscape
- Addresses epistemological questions about what simulation can and cannot prove

## Results

- Identifies three roles for simulation: theory generation, exploring consequences of assumptions, generating hypotheses for empirical testing
- Artificial societies can produce surprising macro-level patterns from simple micro-level rules (emergence)
- Key insight: if agents with simple rules produce patterns matching empirical data, the rules are "sufficient" (not necessary) explanations
- Distinguishes between simulation as tool for understanding vs. simulation as prediction engine

## Limitations

- Pre-LLM: agents have hand-coded behavioral rules, not learned behavior
- 2005 perspective predates the generative ABM revolution
- Epistemological questions (what does a simulation "explain"?) remain unresolved

## Open questions

- Do LLM-based generative agents change the epistemological status of artificial society models?
- Can emergence in LLM simulations be traced back to specific training data patterns?
- What is "sufficient explanation" in a world where agent behavior is learned rather than specified?

## My take

Foundational for understanding the pre-LLM tradition. The "third mode of science" framing is still the best justification for simulation-based research. The emergence and sufficiency concepts reappear in AgentSociety and other generative ABM papers. Reading this alongside the modern generative ABM papers reveals how much — and how little — has changed: the epistemological questions are the same; the agent cognitive architecture is radically different.
