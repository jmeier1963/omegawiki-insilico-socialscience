## Problem

Quantitative indicators (journal impact factors, h-indices, citation counts) are being used in research evaluation in ways that distort research incentives, reward gaming, and fail to capture research quality. How should bibliometrics be used responsibly?

## Key idea

The Leiden Manifesto codifies ten principles for the responsible use of bibliometrics in research evaluation. Quantitative metrics should complement, not replace, expert judgment; should be field-normalized; should not be applied mechanically; and should be transparent.

## Method

- Normative consensus document: 5 authors, internationally recognized bibliometrics experts
- Published in *Nature* 520(7548):429–431 (DOI 10.1038/520429a)
- Led by Diana Hicks, Paul Wouters, Ludo Waltman, Sarah de Rijcke, Ismael Rafols
- Companion to the San Francisco Declaration on Research Assessment (DORA, 2012)

## Results

Ten Leiden Manifesto principles:
1. Quantitative evaluation should support, not replace, qualitative expert assessment
2. Measure performance against the research missions of the institution
3. Protect excellence in locally relevant research
4. Keep data collection and analytical processes open, transparent, and simple
5. Allow those evaluated to verify data and analysis
6. Account for variation by field in publication and citation practices
7. Base assessment on the judgement of the individual researcher for hiring and tenure
8. Avoid misplaced concreteness and false precision
9. Recognize the systemic effects of assessment and indicators
10. Scrutinize indicators regularly and update them

## Limitations

- Normative document — compliance is voluntary
- Does not resolve the fundamental question of how to evaluate research quality without metrics
- Ten principles are sometimes in tension with each other

## Open questions

- How should AI-assisted research outputs be evaluated by research metrics?
- Do AI-generated papers change the baseline statistics that bibliometric norms assume?

## My take

The responsible-metrics counterpart to the Price (1963) quantitative tradition. Essential reading for anyone involved in research evaluation, hiring, or funding decisions. Directly relevant to the Strathern (1997) critique of audit culture and to debates about whether AI-generated papers should count in citation metrics.
