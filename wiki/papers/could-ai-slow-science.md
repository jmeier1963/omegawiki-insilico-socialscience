## Problem

AI is predicted to dramatically accelerate scientific production. But if the bottleneck to scientific *progress* is not production volume, could AI actually slow progress by flooding an already congested pipeline?

## Key idea

The **production-progress paradox**: scientific publication has grown 500-fold since 1900 but measured progress has been constant or slowing. AI accelerates the production of papers (AI adopters publish 3x more, get 5x more citations) but exacerbates the real bottleneck — evaluation, integration, and synthesis. AI is "adding lanes to a highway when the slowdown is caused by a toll booth."

## Method

- Essay from Princeton Center for Information Technology Policy (CITP)
- 2025
- Draws on bibliometric data and science-of-science research
- Cites Science magazine research on AI adopters vs non-adopters

## Results

- AI adopters: 3x publications, 5x citations, faster career advancement — but less interconnected literature
- Science is "shrinking": AI-driven work circles the same crowded problems
- Real bottlenecks are evaluation and synthesis, not idea generation
- Policy makers accelerating production while ignoring true bottlenecks

## Limitations

- Essay/opinion format; no original data
- "Progress" measurement is contested
- AI may eventually address evaluation bottlenecks too (e.g., automated peer review, synthesis agents)

## Open questions

- Can AI address the bottleneck-side (review, synthesis, integration) rather than just the production side?
- How do different scientific fields differ in their production-vs-progress dynamics?

## My take

One of the most important counterarguments to naive AI-accelerates-science optimism. The production-progress paradox is empirically grounded and the "adding lanes to a highway" analogy is the clearest framing of the structural problem. Essential reading alongside the optimistic [[ai-driven-scientific-discovery]] papers to understand the actual risk landscape.
