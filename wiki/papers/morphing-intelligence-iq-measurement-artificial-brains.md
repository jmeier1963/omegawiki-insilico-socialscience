## Problem

What is "intelligence," and how has its definition transformed from genetic IQ testing to epigenetic plasticity to artificial intelligence — and what are the political and philosophical stakes of this transformation?

## Key idea

Intelligence has undergone three crucial metamorphoses: (1) empirical/genetic intelligence measurable by IQ tests; (2) epigenetic/plastic intelligence shaped by experience (neural plasticity); (3) artificial intelligence that simulates and potentially surpasses human cognition. Malabou argues a **dialogue between human and cybernetic intelligence** offers the best means to build a democratic future.

## Method

- Philosophical/theoretical book by Catherine Malabou (Kingston University, Paris Nanterre)
- Originally published in French as "Métamorphoses de l'intelligence" (2017)
- Translated by Carolyn Shread; Columbia University Press 2019
- 198 pages; part of the Wellek Library Lectures series

## Results

- The three epochs of intelligence: genetic fixism → epigenetic plasticity → artificial simulation
- AI represents a "fourth blow to human narcissism" (alongside Copernicus, Darwin, Freud)
- Neither purely deterministic (genetic) nor purely constructivist (plasticity) — AI opens a third epistemological space
- Political argument: intelligence democratization requires engaging with, not rejecting, artificial intelligence

## Limitations

- Continental philosophy framework — claims are interpretive rather than empirically testable
- Written before ChatGPT/LLM era; "artificial intelligence" discussed is primarily ML/deep learning
- Democratic argument is normative rather than analytical

## Open questions

- How does the LLM revolution change Malabou's three-epoch schema — is generative AI a fourth metamorphosis?
- What does plasticity mean for AI systems that can be fine-tuned?

## My take

A philosophically serious engagement with what "intelligence" means as AI becomes central to human cognition. The "three metamorphoses" framing is a useful scaffold for thinking about AI's place in intellectual history. Relevant as intellectual background for in-silico social science — particularly the question of whether LLM simulation of human opinion is a form of intelligence or imitation.
