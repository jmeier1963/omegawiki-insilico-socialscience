## Problem

Generative AI tools like ChatGPT are being used in manuscript preparation, data analysis, and literature review. How should authorship norms in medical journals respond, given that authorship entails accountability for the work?

## Key idea

The May 2023 update to the ICMJE Recommendations clarifies: AI cannot meet authorship criteria (it cannot take accountability for accuracy and integrity); AI use must be disclosed; AI in peer review is subject to existing confidentiality rules.

## Method

- Normative guidelines update to the existing ICMJE Recommendations for medical journal publishing
- Available: icmje.org/recommendations/
- The ICMJE sets standards followed by thousands of medical journals including NEJM, JAMA, Lancet, BMJ

## Results

Key positions:
1. **AI cannot be an author**: authorship requires the ability to take accountability for the work — AI cannot
2. **Disclosure required**: authors must disclose AI tool use in cover letter, methods, or acknowledgements
3. **Human responsibility**: authors are responsible for verifying the accuracy of all AI-generated content
4. **Peer review**: use of AI tools by reviewers is subject to existing confidentiality obligations (submitting manuscripts to AI tools violates confidentiality)

## Limitations

- Focuses on medical journals; other disciplines have their own norms
- Disclosure requirements are vague ("somewhere in the manuscript")
- Enforcement is at the journal level; no field-wide monitoring mechanism

## Open questions

- As AI capabilities increase, will the accountability criterion become even more important to defend?
- How should disclosure be structured to be informative rather than a checkbox exercise?

## My take

The most widely applicable publishing policy on AI authorship, covering thousands of medical journals. The accountability-based rationale for excluding AI from authorship is sound and consistent with Foucault (1969) on the author function as a site of responsibility. Together with DFG (2023) and Nature (2026 editorial), forms the emerging governance consensus on AI in scholarly publishing.
