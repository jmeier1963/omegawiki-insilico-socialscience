## Problem

Audit procedures in British universities are designed to make research and teaching quality measurable and accountable. But do they achieve their goals, or do they produce perverse effects?

## Key idea

Drawing on anthropology, Strathern argues that audit procedures in British universities produce perverse effects: the very act of measuring performance changes what people do, so "ratings" improve without the underlying quality necessarily improving. This is an early theorization of what is now called Goodhart's Law in academic contexts.

## Method

- Anthropological analysis of British university audit culture
- Published: *European Review* 5(3):305–321 (DOI 10.1002/(SICI)1234-981X(199707)5:3<305::AID-EURO184>3.0.CO;2-4)
- Marilyn Strathern (Cambridge)

## Results

- Audit creates "audit culture": activities are restructured to produce good audit results
- The metric-target becomes the goal, displacing the original goal
- Examples: Research Assessment Exercise (RAE), Teaching Quality Assessment
- This is not gaming or dishonesty — it's a structural consequence of making implicit goals explicit

## Limitations

- Primarily UK-focused; generalizes with care
- Anthropological style; not easily operationalizable
- Does not provide concrete alternatives to audit

## Open questions

- Does AI-augmented research amplify Strathern's dynamic? (If AI helps you improve metrics faster than substance)
- How does the Leiden Manifesto (Hicks 2015) address or fail to address Strathern's problem?

## My take

The classic anthropological statement of Goodhart's Law in academia. "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." Strathern shows this is not mere hypocrisy — it's a structural feature of rendering implicit practices explicit. Highly relevant to debates about AI-assisted research inflating bibliometrics without improving substance. The Leiden Manifesto (Hicks 2015) is the normative response.
