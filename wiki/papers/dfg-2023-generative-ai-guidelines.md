## Problem

Generative AI tools (ChatGPT, DALL-E, etc.) are being used in DFG-funded research. What is the appropriate governance framework for their use, particularly regarding authorship and peer review?

## Key idea

The DFG's first official position on generative AI in research: AI tools may be used with mandatory transparency disclosure; their use in peer review is prohibited due to confidentiality requirements; only human natural persons may be listed as authors.

## Method

- Policy statement by DFG Presidium
- Published: September 21, 2023 (Stellungnahme Nr. 39; Pressemitteilung)
- Available: dfg.de/en/service/press/press-releases/2023/press-release-no-39

## Results

Key positions:
1. AI tools may be used in DFG-funded research (no blanket prohibition)
2. Use must be disclosed transparently in the publication
3. AI cannot be named as an author — authorship requires human accountability
4. AI use in peer review (reviewing grant proposals and manuscripts) is prohibited due to confidentiality obligations
5. Researchers bear full responsibility for AI-generated content they include in their work

## Limitations

- First statement; superseded by the 2025 update (DFG 2025) which permits AI in peer review under conditions
- Does not specify what "transparent disclosure" entails in practice
- Focused on DFG-funded German research; other funding bodies have different policies

## Open questions

- Is prohibiting AI authorship sufficient to preserve accountability, or does more detailed disclosure framework needed?
- How do researchers ensure AI-generated content is accurate when they have limited ability to verify it?

## My take

A responsible initial response that prioritized caution. The prohibition on AI in peer review reflected appropriate concern for confidentiality but proved difficult to enforce (Liang et al. 2024 found widespread use anyway). Updated by DFG (2025) which permits AI assistance in review under conditions. Companion to ICMJE (2023) which took similar positions in biomedical publishing.
