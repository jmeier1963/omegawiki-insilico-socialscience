## Problem

Traditional science operates by hypothesis → experiment → theory. But in the petabyte era, correlation patterns found in massive datasets seem sufficient to answer scientific questions without hypotheses or models. Has the scientific method become obsolete?

## Key idea

The petabyte era of massive data, combined with statistical correlation tools, makes scientific hypothesis formation unnecessary — patterns found in data are sufficient, causation no longer needs to be sought. "Correlation is enough." This provocation sparked sustained debate about the role of theory in data-driven science.

## Method

- Magazine article: *Wired*, June 23, 2008
- Chris Anderson (then editor of Wired, author of *The Long Tail*)
- Not a peer-reviewed academic piece — a provocation

## Results

Main claims:
- Google and similar data systems find patterns too complex for any human theory
- With enough data, the numbers speak for themselves
- "We can stop looking for models. We can analyze the data without hypotheses about what it might show"
- "Correlation supersedes causation, and science can advance even without coherent models, unified theories, or really any mechanistic explanation at all"

## Limitations

- Journalistic provocation, not scholarship — no systematic argument
- The claim about correlation superseding causation is philosophically untenable (Pearl & Mackenzie 2018 directly refutes it)
- Google Translate example (mentioned in the article) is from a different paradigm than scientific discovery
- Subsequent scientific experience has not borne out the most extreme version of the claim

## Open questions

- With LLMs, has Anderson's vision been partially vindicated? Can models "know" without theories?
- What does the "end of theory" claim look like in the specific context of AI-driven science today?

## My take

The ur-provocation for data-driven science debates. Anderson's claim is too strong but not entirely wrong: modern AI does produce useful predictions without explicit theories. The distinction that Anderson elides — between prediction and understanding — is exactly what the Humphreys, Sullivan, and Messeri & Crockett papers work to clarify. Pearl & Mackenzie (2018) provide the clearest philosophical refutation. Still worth reading as historical context.
