## Problem

AI tools are being rapidly adopted in scientific research, but we lack large-scale empirical evidence of how widely, in which fields, and with what outcomes AI is being used in published science.

## Key idea

Large-scale bibliometric analysis of ~75 million publications finds that AI-assisted papers are significantly more likely to become "hit papers" (top 1% by citations), but AI adoption is uneven across fields, institutions, and geographies — raising concerns that AI amplifies existing scientific inequalities.

## Method

- Analysis of ~75 million papers from Web of Science and other databases
- Identified AI-assisted papers using keyword and method-section analysis
- Measured citation outcomes, hit-paper rates, collaboration patterns
- Geographic and institutional breakdowns of AI adoption
- Published in *Nature Human Behaviour* 8(12):2281–2292 (DOI 10.1038/s41562-024-02020-5)

## Results

- AI-assisted papers are ~5x more likely to become hit papers
- AI adoption strongly concentrated in: US/Europe/China (less so Global South), elite institutions, ML/data-science-adjacent fields
- The productivity advantage of AI is not equally distributed — it may widen the gap between high- and low-resource researchers
- Field variation is large: genomics, materials science, NLP benefit most; humanities/qualitative social science least

## Limitations

- "AI-assisted" classification based on text signals — may misclassify some papers
- Correlation with hit-paper status doesn't establish causation (AI may be adopted first in already-productive labs)
- Long-term effects on scientific diversity not captured

## Open questions

- Does AI assistance improve scientific quality or just impact metrics?
- Will AI tools eventually democratize research, or will early adopter advantages persist?
- How do uneven adoption patterns interact with Longino's (1990) conditions for scientific objectivity?

## My take

The most comprehensive empirical study of AI adoption in science to date. The inequality finding is the most important result: AI is an amplifier, not an equalizer, at least in the current early adoption phase. This directly supports Longino (1990) and Messeri & Crockett (2024) concerns about scientific monocultures.
