## Problem

Frontier AI companies are using AI to accelerate their own AI R&D. How far could this automation go, how fast, and what strategic and governance challenges does it create?

## Key idea

Synthesizes expert consensus from a July 2025 workshop: AI R&D is already being automated by frontier labs and is increasing. Scenarios exist in which automation accelerates to an "intelligence explosion," largely inside companies and out of public view — a potential source of major strategic surprise. Governance action is warranted now.

## Method

- Expert workshop held July 2025 (Georgetown CSET, ~20 participants)
- Distills points of consensus and competing "trajectory models"
- Authors: Helen Toner, Kendrea Beers, Steve Newman, Saif Khan, et al.
- Published January 2026

## Results

- **Consensus**: Frontier labs already use AI in AI R&D; usage is increasing; new models deployed internally before public release
- **Trajectory models** include: (1) productivity-multiplier "explosion," (2) "fizzle" plateau, (3) Amdahl's law bottleneck (human bottleneck limits acceleration), (4) "expanding pie" (humans move to harder-to-automate tasks)
- **Key risk**: automation could progress inside closed-lab environments, out of public view, creating strategic surprise
- **Core message**: intelligence explosion is plausible enough to justify governance action now

## Limitations

- Expert elicitation only — no empirical measurement of current automation levels
- "Intelligence explosion" remains highly uncertain and contested
- Report reflects workshop consensus; may underrepresent minority expert views

## Open questions

- What governance mechanisms could maintain visibility into closed-lab AI R&D automation?
- Which trajectory model is empirically most consistent with current lab behavior?
- How do compute limits interact with software-automation trajectories?

## My take

An important policy document: CSET is credible and cautious, so this represents mainstream security-community concern about AI self-improvement. The "largely inside companies, out of public view" framing is the key governance gap. Directly relevant to questions about accelerating scientific discovery via AI agents.
