## Problem

What exactly is opaque about deep neural networks, and why does it matter for scientific knowledge? A more precise characterization of opacity types is needed to assess when and how AI-produced results constitute scientific knowledge.

## Key idea

Deep neural networks are opaque along two distinct dimensions: (1) internal opacity — inaccessibility of the internal representations and their meaning; (2) relational opacity — inability to articulate the mapping from input to output in scientifically interpretable terms. The combination creates a "predicament": successful discovery can outpace the development of concepts needed for understanding.

## Method

- Philosophical analysis of deep learning architectures and their epistemology
- Published: *Minds and Machines* 32:43–75 (DOI 10.1007/s11023-021-09569-4)
- Florian J. Boge (Bergische Universität Wuppertal)

## Results

Two dimensions of opacity:
- **Internal opacity**: the learned representations (weights, activations) are not interpretable in human conceptual terms
- **Relational opacity**: the input-output mapping cannot be expressed in scientifically relevant language (causal mechanisms, structural equations, etc.)

The "deep learning predicament": even when a DL model produces a successful unsupervised discovery, scientists lack the conceptual vocabulary to articulate what was discovered in mechanistic terms. Discovery precedes understanding.

## Limitations

- The two dimensions may not be fully independent
- Does not fully engage with Sullivan's linking assumptions framework
- Focused on the most opaque end of the spectrum — does not address interpretable ML

## Open questions

- Is the "predicament" resolvable, or is it an inherent feature of DL-based discovery?
- Does AlphaFold represent a case where the predicament is partially resolved (structural understanding follows from structural prediction)?

## My take

The most precise philosophical treatment of what "opacity" means in DL. The two-dimension framework is more useful than the simple "transparent/opaque" binary. The "predicament" concept captures something important: current DL-driven science may systematically produce discoveries that we cannot yet understand. See also Alvarado (2022) for agent-independent opacity.
