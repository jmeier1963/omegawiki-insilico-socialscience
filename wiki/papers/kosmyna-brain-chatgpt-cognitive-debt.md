## Problem

Does relying on ChatGPT for cognitive tasks like essay writing reduce the neural engagement associated with learning and deep processing? Does repeated AI use accumulate a "cognitive debt" that persists even when AI assistance is removed?

## Key idea

Using EEG to measure brain network connectivity during essay writing, participants who relied on ChatGPT showed weaker and less distributed neural networks than those using internet search or writing unaided. The pattern worsened across sessions and persisted when LLM users were switched to writing without AI — consistent with accumulating cognitive debt.

## Method

- Participants: 3 groups (ChatGPT, internet search, brain-only)
- 4 sessions of essay writing under respective conditions; LLM group switched to unaided in final session
- EEG measurement of neural connectivity (functional connectivity across brain regions)
- MIT Media Lab (Kosmyna, Hauptmann, Yuan, Situ, Liao, Beresnitzky, Braunstein, Maes)
- arXiv preprint 2506.08872 (not yet peer-reviewed)

## Results

- ChatGPT group: weakest neural connectivity across all sessions
- Effect worsened across repeated sessions (accumulation)
- Persisted when ChatGPT group was switched to unaided writing (session 4)
- Brain-only group maintained strong connectivity throughout
- Self-ownership of ideas rated lowest in ChatGPT group

## Limitations

- Preprint — not yet peer-reviewed
- Small sample size (typical for EEG studies)
- The "cognitive debt" framing is metaphorical — EEG connectivity ≠ cognitive debt directly
- Essay quality comparison not reported

## Open questions

- Does the cognitive debt reverse with extended AI-free practice?
- Do different AI assistance modes (AI that explains vs. AI that generates) produce different patterns?
- What are the long-term academic outcomes for students who heavily rely on AI writing tools?

## My take

The first neural-level evidence for what Carr (2010) predicted about internet use and what Risko & Gilbert (2016) theorized about cognitive offloading. The session-to-session accumulation and persistence after AI removal is the most alarming finding — suggesting the effect is not just about the immediate cognitive state but about changing learning trajectories. Should be taken seriously despite preprint status.
