## Problem

Standard theoretical models of dusty plasmas assume symmetric (reciprocal) inter-particle forces. But experimental behavior does not always match predictions. Can ML extract the actual force laws from experimental data, and do they confirm or refute theoretical assumptions?

## Key idea

A physics-tailored neural network extracts inter-particle force laws from experimental dusty plasma data with >99% accuracy, discovering that standard theoretical assumptions about non-reciprocal forces are incorrect — one of the few cases of AI genuinely discovering new physical laws rather than just predicting outcomes.

## Method

- Physics-tailored neural network architecture incorporating known physical constraints (symmetries, dimensionality)
- Applied to experimental dusty plasma data from Emory University lab
- Published: *PNAS* (DOI 10.1073/pnas.2505725122)
- First author: Wentao Yu (Emory; now Caltech)
- Note: user cited as "Wang et al." — first author is Yu (Wentao Yu); "Wang" may refer to a co-author

## Results

- Neural network extracts force laws from experimental data with >99% accuracy
- Discovered that standard non-reciprocal force assumptions in dusty plasma theory are incorrect
- The ML-discovered force law makes novel predictions confirmed by subsequent experiments
- Demonstrates that physics-informed ML can make genuine theoretical discoveries

## Limitations

- Specific to dusty plasma systems — generalization to other force-law discovery requires care
- Physics-tailored approach requires domain knowledge to build in constraints
- PNAS 2025 — relatively recent; independent replication not yet established

## Open questions

- Can physics-tailored ML discover force laws in other complex plasma, fluid, or quantum systems?
- What is the relationship between force law recovery and theoretical understanding (de Regt)?

## My take

One of the cleanest examples of AI discovering genuinely new physics. The key is the "physics-tailored" aspect: incorporating symmetry and physical constraints into the ML architecture makes the learned representations interpretable and the discovery verifiable. Together with Iten et al. (2020) and Udrescu/Tegmark (2020), represents the strongest category of AI-driven physical discovery.
