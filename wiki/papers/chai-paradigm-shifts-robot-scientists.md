## Problem

Are autonomous AI systems (robot scientists) capable of driving genuine paradigm shifts in science, or do they accelerate normal science without creating revolutionary change? What distinguishes AI-driven discovery from automation of existing methods?

## Key idea

Argues that robot scientists and autonomous laboratory systems represent a potential Kuhnian paradigm shift in how science is conducted (the *process* of science), even if individual discoveries remain cumulative. Distinguishes between AI as a tool within existing paradigms vs. AI changing the paradigm of scientific practice itself.

## Method

- Perspective/analysis paper
- Published: *Science Bulletin* (DOI 10.1016/j.scib.2024.09.029), 2024
- Reviews examples of autonomous laboratory systems (Szymanski 2023, Burger 2020) and AI discovery systems

## Results

- Robot scientists represent a shift in the *means* of scientific production, not necessarily the conceptual content of science
- The paradigm shift is in scientific *practice*: human-in-the-loop → AI-in-the-loop
- This is analogous to prior instrumentational paradigm shifts (sequencing, mass spectrometry)
- Does not yet constitute Kuhnian "revolutionary science" in conceptual terms — remains cumulative in what it discovers

## Limitations

- Perspective paper — limited empirical grounding
- Uses "paradigm shift" loosely, conflating methodological and conceptual senses
- Predictions about future autonomous discovery remain speculative

## Open questions

- At what point does AI-driven discovery become genuinely revolutionary rather than accelerated normal science?
- Does Krauss (2024) debunking of paradigm shifts apply equally to methodological vs. conceptual revolutions?

## My take

An interesting companion to Krauss (2024): where Krauss argues paradigm shifts are rare in the history of science's *content*, Chai argues the *process* of science is undergoing a genuine paradigm shift. This distinction between first-order and meta-level scientific change is useful. Pairs well with Szymanski (2023) and Burger (2020) as concrete examples of the methodological shift being described.
