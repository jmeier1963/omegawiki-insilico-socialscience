## Problem

Standard accounts of epistemic opacity in computational systems treat it as agent-relative: a system is opaque to an agent because that agent lacks certain cognitive capacities. But some forms of opacity in ML/simulations cannot be resolved by improving any human's cognition. What is this deeper form of opacity?

## Key idea

Some forms of epistemic opacity in ML and simulation systems are agent-independent — they do not arise from and cannot be resolved by any improvement to the human investigator's cognitive capacities. This structural opacity is categorically different from ordinary agent-relative opacity and raises distinct epistemological challenges.

## Method

- Philosophical analysis
- Distinguishes agent-relative opacity (∃ agent with sufficient capacity to resolve it) from agent-independent opacity (no possible agent can resolve it)
- PhilSci-Archive preprint (ID 19384) + journal publication in *Science and Engineering Ethics* (DOI 10.1007/s11948-023-00451-3)
- Ramón Alvarado

## Results

- Agent-independent opacity arises from the combinatorial complexity of high-dimensional learned representations — no human could, even in principle, track all the relevant interactions
- This is structurally novel — traditional transparency solutions (interpretability, explanations) cannot fully resolve it
- Implications for AI accountability and governance: some AI decisions may be permanently opaque

## Limitations

- "In principle" is philosophically loaded — it's unclear what cognitive capacities are being imagined
- May overstate opacity: approximations and partial explanations may be practically sufficient
- Does not engage fully with Sullivan's linking-assumptions alternative

## Open questions

- Is agent-independent opacity in AI ethically problematic, or is computational reliabilism (Duran & Formanek) sufficient?
- Does this distinction matter for legal and regulatory frameworks for AI?

## My take

An important sharpening of the opacity debate. The agent-relative/agent-independent distinction cuts through common confusions. If correct, it means some AI systems are permanently opaque in a deep sense — no amount of interpretability research can fully resolve the opacity. This has significant implications for high-stakes AI deployment and accountability.
