## Problem

Published scientific results may not be reproducible. But how widespread is the problem? The field lacked systematic empirical data on the actual replication rate of psychological research findings.

## Key idea

A consortium of ~270 researchers (Open Science Collaboration) directly replicated 100 studies published in three top psychology journals in 2008, using the original authors' materials and pre-registered protocols. The replication rate was assessed by multiple criteria: significance, effect sizes, and subjective assessor judgment.

## Method

- 100 studies sampled from *Psychological Science*, *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Learning, Memory, and Cognition* (2008 issues)
- Pre-registered replications using original materials where possible
- Multiple criteria: p < 0.05 in replication, same direction, effect size comparison
- Large team (Many Labs approach)

## Results

- Only 36–39% of studies produced p < 0.05 in replication (depending on criterion)
- Mean effect size in replications = ~50% of original effect size
- ~68% had effects in the same direction (but many not significant)
- Cognitive psychology replicated better than social psychology

## Limitations

- Replications were exact, not conceptual; some failures may reflect context-sensitivity
- Original study authors sometimes disputed replication procedures
- 2008 sample may not represent contemporary research
- Cannot distinguish true non-reproducibility from methodological failures

## Open questions

- What fraction of published AI/ML results are reproducible? (Kapoor & Narayanan 2023)
- Is the replication crisis worse or better in other fields?
- Does AI-assisted research improve or worsen reproducibility?

## My take

The empirical document that launched the replication crisis into mainstream attention. Its 36% replication rate shocked the field and drove reform (pre-registration, open data, open materials). Directly motivates Mayo (2018) on severity, Kapoor & Narayanan (2023) on ML leakage, and the FAIR principles (Wilkinson 2016). Essential reading for any discussion of scientific reliability.
