## Problem

Does ChatGPT and its successors represent genuine machine intelligence, or a sophisticated but fundamentally limited form of pattern matching that will "degrade science and debase ethics"?

## Key idea

ChatGPT is "a lumbering statistical engine for pattern matching" that fundamentally differs from human cognition. Human language competence is not statistical inference over data — it creates explanations, identifies counterfactuals, and distinguishes the possible from the impossible. The deepest flaw of current AI is the inability to say what is *not* the case.

## Method

- Opinion essay: Noam Chomsky (MIT/University of Arizona), Ian Roberts (Cambridge), Jeffrey Watumull (Oceanit AI)
- Published March 8, 2023, New York Times
- Argument from linguistics and cognitive science

## Results

- LLMs produce "approximate" language through brute statistical correlation, not through productive rule-governed competence
- The human mind operates on minimal data with maximal generativity; LLMs invert this
- Moral reasoning requires distinguishing what is from what ought to be — LLMs lack this capacity
- "Useful in some narrow domains" but cannot constitute intelligence

## Limitations

- Written early in GPT-4 era; many capabilities have expanded since
- Critique is conceptual/philosophical — does not engage empirically with model capabilities
- "Statistical engine" framing may undercharacterize learned representations

## Open questions

- Does scaling change the fundamental nature of LLM reasoning or just its coverage?
- Can counterfactual and modal reasoning be taught through RLHF or similar methods?

## My take

Chomsky's critique remains the most articulate philosophical statement of LLM skepticism. The key argument — that intelligence requires the ability to say what is *not* the case — is genuinely distinct from capability-based critiques. Relevant counterpoint to optimistic AI-science papers: the claim that LLMs reason is contested at the conceptual level, not just empirically.
