## Problem

Can AI help resolve open mathematical problems at scale? The Erdős Problems database contains 700+ conjectures labeled "Open" but many may be solvable with sufficient search.

## Key idea

Using Gemini to systematically evaluate 700 conjectures from the Erdős Problems database via hybrid human-AI workflow. AI performs natural-language verification to narrow the search space; human experts validate correctness and novelty. Result: 13 previously open problems resolved — 5 via autonomous solutions, 8 via identification of existing literature.

## Method

- Gemini evaluates 700+ open conjectures via natural-language reasoning
- AI narrows candidates to plausible resolutions
- Human expert panel evaluates correctness and originality
- Hybrid pipeline: AI as verifier/filter, humans as final judges

## Results

- 13 problems resolved: 5 novel autonomous solutions, 8 existing-literature discoveries
- Key insight: many "open" problems are open due to knowledge silos, not intrinsic difficulty
- "Subconscious plagiarism" identified as a risk: AI may reproduce known results without citation

## Limitations

- 8 of 13 resolutions relied on finding existing literature — AI as search tool, not theorem prover
- Novel mathematical proofs still require human expert validation
- Risk of "subconscious plagiarism" not yet solved

## Open questions

- Can AI produce genuinely novel mathematical proofs not derivable from training data?
- How do we distinguish AI rediscovery from AI novelty?

## My take

The most interesting result is the meta-finding: many open problems persist due to knowledge fragmentation (different communities independently hold relevant results) rather than fundamental mathematical difficulty. AI as a cross-domain literature synthesizer may be more valuable than AI as a theorem prover. The 5 novel solutions are impressive but their mathematical depth relative to known open problems is unclear from the abstract.
