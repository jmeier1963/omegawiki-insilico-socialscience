## Definition

Eisenstein's 1979 two-volume Cambridge UP study argues that Gutenberg's printing press was a transformative historical force in its own right — not merely a neutral conduit for ideas — because it enabled standardization, wide dissemination, and accumulation of knowledge at scales impossible in manuscript culture, thereby catalyzing the Renaissance, Reformation, and Scientific Revolution.

## Intuition

Before print, knowledge was unreliable, local, and constantly modified in copying. Print enabled stable, standardized, widely reproducible texts. This wasn't just faster copying — it changed what knowledge itself could be: cumulative, correctable across sites, capable of reaching simultaneous readerships. The printing press didn't just spread ideas; it changed the nature of knowledge.

## Formal notation

No formal notation. Key mechanisms of change:
- **Standardization**: identical copies replace scribal variation
- **Dissemination**: simultaneous wide distribution across geographies
- **Fixity**: stable text enables cumulative correction and reference
- **Amplification of error**: but also amplification of discovery and correction

## Key variants

- **McLuhan (1962)**: *The Gutenberg Galaxy* — media-determinist predecessor
- **Johns (1998)**: *The Nature of the Book* — challenges Eisenstein's fixity thesis
- **Briggs & Burke (2002)**: broader social history of communication

## Known limitations

- Technological determinism critique: overemphasizes the press as autonomous agent
- The timing of effects (Renaissance, Scientific Revolution) is contested
- Fixity thesis is challenged by the heterogeneity of early print

## Open problems

- Is AI/LLM a similarly transformative communication technology? What stabilizes or destabilizes knowledge?
- Does AI-generated content undermine the fixity and reliability that print enabled?

## Relevance to active research

Canonical reference for analogical arguments about AI as transformative technology. Cited in debates about whether AI represents a comparable inflection point to the printing press. Also relevant to Shumailov (2024) model collapse — synthetic data erodes the stable, reliable corpus that print (and the internet) created.
