## Definition

Carr's 2010 W.W. Norton book argues that the internet's hyperlinked, distraction-heavy structure rewires the brain through neuroplasticity, eroding the capacity for deep, sustained reading and contemplative thought in favor of shallow, rapid information-skimming.

## Intuition

Media shape how we think, not just what we think about. The internet promotes rapid switching, multitasking, and scanning — cognitive habits that neuroplasticity consolidates at the expense of the sustained attention required for deep reading, complex reasoning, and long-form thought.

## Formal notation

No formal notation. Key empirical claims:
- Google-style information retrieval promotes skimming over reading
- Hyperlinks interrupt linear attention even when not clicked
- Neuroplasticity means habitual modes of reading reshape neural circuits
- Historical parallel: Gutenberg's printing press created the "deep reader" by demanding sustained linear attention

## Key variants

- **Risko & Gilbert (2016)**: cognitive offloading as adaptive — partly in tension with Carr
- **Kosmyna et al. (2025)**: empirical EEG evidence of "cognitive debt" from AI reliance
- **Bauerlein (2008)**: *The Dumbest Generation* — related argument about digital culture

## Known limitations

- Empirical evidence for neuroplastic rewiring from internet use is contested
- Carr relies heavily on anecdote and popular neuroscience
- May conflate correlation with causation in historical comparisons
- Technophobic reading is possible but not necessary

## Open problems

- Does AI assistance cause deeper cognitive offloading than internet use?
- Kosmyna et al. (2025) provides the empirical test Carr lacked — do the EEG results support his thesis?

## Relevance to active research

Provides conceptual framing for Kosmyna et al. (2025) and Risko & Gilbert (2016) debates about cognitive offloading to AI. Bastani et al. (2024) AI learning harm and Messeri & Crockett (2024) illusions of understanding are both consistent with extended versions of Carr's thesis.
