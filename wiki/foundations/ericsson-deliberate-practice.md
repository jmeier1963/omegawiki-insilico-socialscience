## Definition

Ericsson, Krampe, and Tesch-Römer's 1993 *Psychological Review* paper (vol. 100, no. 3, pp. 363–406; DOI 10.1037/0033-295X.100.3.363) argues that expert performance is primarily the product of sustained, effortful, feedback-rich practice specifically designed to improve performance ("deliberate practice"), not innate talent.

## Intuition

Elite violinists differ from good violinists primarily in accumulated hours of deliberate practice — not in natural talent. Deliberate practice means: identifying specific performance gaps, practicing specifically to close those gaps, getting immediate feedback, and pushing just beyond current ability. Casual repetition (playing through familiar pieces) doesn't count.

## Formal notation

No formal notation. Key concepts:
- **Deliberate practice**: effortful, focused, feedback-guided improvement practice with explicit performance goals
- **Accumulated deliberate practice hours**: the primary predictor of expert-level performance (>10,000 hours for international-level competition)
- **Naive practice**: repetition without the deliberate structure (does not improve performance)

## Key variants

- **Gladwell (2008)**: popular "10,000 hours" claim (oversimplification of Ericsson)
- **Ericsson (2016)**: *Peak* — revised and extended for general audience
- **Macnamara et al. (2014)**: meta-analysis finding deliberate practice explains 26% of variance — less than Ericsson claims

## Known limitations

- Domain-specific: applies best to skill domains with clear feedback criteria (music, chess, sports)
- Replications have found smaller effects in many domains
- Does not address creative, open-ended domains without clear performance feedback

## Open problems

- Does AI assistance enhance or circumvent the need for deliberate practice?
- Bastani et al. (2024) showing AI use prevents exam learning is the educational version of this question

## Relevance to active research

Background for debates about AI and skill acquisition. Bastani et al. (2024) AI harm learning, Kosmyna et al. (2025) cognitive debt, and Risko & Gilbert (2016) cognitive offloading all engage with what happens when AI performs cognitive work that normally builds expertise via deliberate practice.
