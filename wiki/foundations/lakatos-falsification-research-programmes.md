## Definition

Lakatos's 1970 essay (in Lakatos & Musgrave, *Criticism and the Growth of Knowledge*) replaces Popper's naive falsificationism with the "scientific research programme" — a sequence of theories sharing an irrefutable hard core and a modifiable protective belt of auxiliary hypotheses.

## Intuition

Scientists don't abandon a theory the moment it faces a counterexample; they adjust auxiliary assumptions to protect the core. Whether this is rational depends on whether the programme is *progressive* (generating novel confirmed predictions) or *degenerative* (only explaining failures after the fact).

## Formal notation

A research programme R consists of:
- **Hard core (H)**: unfalsifiable core commitments (protected by methodological decision)
- **Protective belt (P)**: auxiliary hypotheses, initial conditions, observation theories — all modifiable
- **Positive heuristic**: guidelines for how to modify P
- **Negative heuristic**: prohibition on applying *modus tollens* to H

Progress: a programme is **progressive** if theory Tₙ₊₁ predicts novel facts that Tₙ did not, and those facts are confirmed.

## Key variants

- **Kuhn**: paradigms (less rationalistic account of science)
- **Laudan (1977)**: research traditions as further refinement
- **Feyerabend**: rejects all methodological rules

## Known limitations

- "Novel fact" is ambiguous — when is a prediction genuinely novel?
- Does not specify how long a degenerating programme should be tolerated
- History of science examples (Newton, Prout) are contested
- Gives post-hoc rationalizations more easily than prospective guidance

## Open problems

- Do AI-driven research programmes have "hard cores"? What is unfalsifiable?
- How do we evaluate degeneration/progressiveness in automated research pipelines (agent-laboratory papers)?

## Relevance to active research

Provides vocabulary for evaluating AI-driven research traditions: are current LLM-for-science programmes progressive or degenerative? Also relevant to reproducibility debates (Open Science Collaboration 2015, Kapoor & Narayanan 2023).
