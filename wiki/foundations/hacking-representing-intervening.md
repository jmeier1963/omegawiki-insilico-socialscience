## Definition

Hacking's 1983 introductory text in philosophy of science argues that the realism debate cannot be settled by theoretical considerations alone. The strongest case for scientific realism comes from experimental practice: when scientists routinely *intervene* using an entity (spray electrons, inject viruses), they treat it as real — grounding "entity realism" in practice rather than theory.

## Intuition

If you can use electrons to measure the charge on a superconducting sphere, electrons are real — regardless of whether quantum electrodynamics is the true theory of electrons. The ability to manipulate is stronger evidence of reality than inferential success of theories.

## Formal notation

No formal notation. Key distinction:
- **Representing**: theoretical claims about unobservable entities (the contested territory)
- **Intervening**: experimental manipulation using those entities (the pragmatic ground of realism)

## Key variants

- **van Fraassen (1980)**: constructive empiricism — anti-realism about unobservables
- **Cartwright (1983)**: laws of physics lie; real are capacities used in experiments
- **Psillos**: structural realism as alternative

## Known limitations

- Entity realism is silent on theoretical claims (you can be realist about electrons but agnostic about QED)
- "Intervention" criterion is hard to apply to entities that resist manipulation (dark matter, early universe)

## Open problems

- Can AI-discovered entities (e.g., protein structures in AlphaFold) be given realist status via Hacking's intervention criterion?
- Does "using" AI outputs in subsequent experimental interventions make AI-inferred entities "real"?

## Relevance to active research

Relevant to debates about whether AI-derived knowledge constitutes genuine scientific knowledge. AlphaFold (Jumper 2021) predictions are now used to intervene in biological systems — suggesting Hacking's criterion is met. Also relevant to Szymanski (2023) autonomous lab experiments.
