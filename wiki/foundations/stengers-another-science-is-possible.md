## Definition

Stengers's 2018 Polity Press manifesto (translated from French *Une autre science est possible*, 2013) argues that contemporary science is deeply entangled with and distorted by market pressures, performance metrics, and the demand for rapid deliverables. It calls for a "slow science" that takes time for genuine curiosity, dissent, and ecological and social responsibility.

## Intuition

Science under neoliberal conditions is forced to produce fast, fundable, publishable results. This systematically excludes slow questions (ones whose value isn't immediately legible to funders), dissenting voices (who risk career penalty), and risky speculation (which doesn't produce safe outputs). A different science would prioritize curiosity over productivity.

## Formal notation

No formal notation. Key claims:
- **Performance metrics** (publications, citations, impact factors) distort research incentives (see Hicks et al. 2015)
- **Acceleration** (Rosa 2005) structurally prevents deep inquiry
- **Cosmopolitical proposal**: slow down before deciding what counts as legitimate science

## Key variants

- **Rosa (2005)**: *Beschleunigung* — theoretical framework for what Stengers resists
- **Hicks et al. (2015)**: Leiden Manifesto — normative guidelines for metrics reform
- **Could AI slow science?** (CITP 2025) — empirical parallel argument

## Known limitations

- "Slow science" is a normative vision without concrete institutional proposals
- Risk of romanticizing pre-modern or pre-institutional science
- Tension between arguing for slower science and engaging with fast AI-driven discovery

## Open problems

- Does AI speed up science in ways that exacerbate what Stengers identifies?
- Can "slow science" values be institutionally protected while AI-driven discovery accelerates?

## Relevance to active research

Provides normative counterpoint to AI-as-accelerator narratives. Relevant to DFG (2025) policy debates, Messeri & Crockett (2024) on epistemic monocultures, and generally to any discussion of how AI-driven acceleration affects research quality.
