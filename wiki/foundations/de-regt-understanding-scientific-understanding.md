## Definition

de Regt's 2017 monograph (Oxford UP) defends scientific understanding as a legitimate epistemic goal and proposes the Contextual Theory of Understanding (CIT): a scientist has understanding of a phenomenon if they can use a theory to construct qualitatively correct predictions without performing exact calculations, and the theory is intelligible to them.

## Intuition

Understanding is not just knowing that something is true — it is being able to "work with" a theory in a skilled way. Intelligibility is the cluster of qualities (visualizability, mathematical tractability, causal familiarity) that makes a theory usable for skilled prediction. This is historically and contextually variable.

## Formal notation

**CIT criterion**: a scientist S has understanding of a phenomenon P using theory T iff:
1. T has sufficient intelligibility for S (given S's skills and the available tools)
2. S can use T to construct qualitatively correct predictions of P without exact calculation

Intelligibility is not intrinsic to a theory but depends on the scientist's conceptual toolkit and community standards.

## Key variants

- **Friedman (1974)**: understanding = reduction in the number of brute facts (unification)
- **Kitcher (1989)**: unification account
- **Strevens (2008)**: kairetic account — understanding requires grasping causal structure

## Known limitations

- "Intelligibility" is vague and hard to operationalize
- CIT may be too permissive: could a scientist have understanding via black-box models that they can use but not interpret?
- Focuses on individual scientists; underspecifies social dimensions

## Open problems

- Do AI models provide understanding in de Regt's sense? Sullivan (2022) directly addresses this.
- If scientists can use AlphaFold predictions without understanding the model, do they understand protein folding?

## Relevance to active research

The central reference for Sullivan (2022), Messeri & Crockett (2024), and Boge (2022) discussions of whether AI models provide genuine scientific understanding. The intelligibility criterion is the key concept at stake in opacity debates.
