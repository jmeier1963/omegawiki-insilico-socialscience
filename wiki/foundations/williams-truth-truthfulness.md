## Definition

Williams's 2002 Princeton UP monograph uses a Nietzschean genealogical method to simultaneously defend the importance of truth against postmodern skepticism and criticize naive correspondence theories. Two fundamental virtues of truthfulness — Sincerity (asserting what one believes) and Accuracy (believing carefully investigated claims) — are identified as essential to any functioning epistemic community.

## Intuition

We cannot simply define truth and then deduce that truthfulness matters — we need to show how practices of truth-telling evolved and why they matter practically. The virtues of Sincerity (not lying) and Accuracy (not asserting carelessly) are prior to and independent of any particular theory of what truth is. Without them, no cooperative epistemic practice is possible.

## Formal notation

No formal notation. Two epistemic virtues:
- **Sincerity**: not asserting what one does not believe; the basis of trust
- **Accuracy**: caring about whether one's beliefs are true; the basis of reliable knowledge

Genealogical method: showing how these virtues arose and why they are functional — not deducing them from a metaphysical theory of truth.

## Key variants

- **Chisholm (1966)**: epistemic virtue theory (earlier)
- **Zagzebski (1996)**: *Virtues of the Mind* — systematic virtue epistemology
- **Frankfurt (2005)**: *On Bullshit* — practical companion

## Known limitations

- Genealogical method is not a vindication — showing how something evolved doesn't show it is justified
- Limited engagement with social epistemology (collective accuracy practices)
- Pre-AI analysis doesn't address machine accuracy or AI-generated falsehoods

## Open problems

- Can AI systems be "sincere"? Do LLMs have beliefs whose accuracy they can care about?
- How do AI-generated hallucinations undermine the social epistemic fabric that depends on sincerity and accuracy?

## Relevance to active research

Provides philosophical vocabulary for AI honesty/truthfulness debates. Relevant to ICMJE (2023) authorship norms, DFG (2023) AI transparency requirements, and general concerns about AI-generated misinformation in science. Also relevant to Smith (2006) peer review critique.
