## Definition

Galison's 1997 monograph (Chicago UP, 955 pp.) argues that 20th-century experimental physics comprises two distinct subcultures — image-based (cloud chambers, bubble chambers, visual tracks) and logic-based (electronic counters, statistical aggregates) — each with its own standards of evidence. Their coexistence and periodic "trading zone" negotiations shape the overall knowledge-making of physics.

## Intuition

High-energy physics is not a unified culture but an "intercalated" system of partially disunified practices. Image and logic traditions have different standards for what counts as a "good event," a "discovery," and even a "result." They persist side by side and negotiate in "trading zones" where partial coordination occurs without full translation.

## Formal notation

No formal notation. Key concepts:
- **Trading zone**: a site where two communities coordinate without sharing full meanings (creole, pidgin, interactional expertise)
- **Intercalation**: the layering of partially independent subcultures that produce local coordination without global unity
- **Disunity of science**: the deliberate methodological incommensurability between image and logic traditions

## Key variants

- **Collins & Evans (2002)**: interactional vs. contributory expertise (extends trading zone)
- **Knorr-Cetina (1999)**: epistemic cultures (parallel empirical argument)

## Known limitations

- Focuses on high-energy physics; generalization to other sciences requires care
- "Trading zone" concept has been stretched to many contexts by subsequent scholars
- 955-page scope makes key arguments hard to extract

## Open problems

- Does AI constitute a new "trading zone" between theoretical physics and computation?
- How do AI tools interact with the image vs. logic traditions in contemporary experimental science?

## Relevance to active research

Provides vocabulary for understanding how AI tools are integrated (or not) across disciplinary subcultures. Directly relevant to debates about AI in experimental science and cross-disciplinary knowledge transfer.
