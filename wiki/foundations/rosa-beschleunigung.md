## Definition

Hartmut Rosa's 2005 Suhrkamp monograph (English: *Social Acceleration*, Columbia UP, 2013) proposes that social acceleration — the increase in the pace of technological production, social change, and the tempo of daily life — is the structural principle of modernity. Acceleration produces alienation when people can no longer meaningfully relate to their activities, relationships, and world.

## Intuition

Modern societies demand faster and faster output while offering less and less time for depth, reflection, and meaningful engagement. This isn't an accident — acceleration is structurally reproduced by the economic system (competition), political institutions (electoral cycles), and cultural patterns (FOMO, presentism). The result is a "frozen" society that races to stand still.

## Formal notation

No formal notation. Three dimensions of acceleration:
- **Technological acceleration**: faster machines, transport, communication
- **Acceleration of social change**: faster turnover of jobs, relationships, knowledge
- **Acceleration of the pace of life**: subjective feeling of insufficient time; compression of experience

Alienation = inability to appropriate one's own life as meaningful despite (or because of) acceleration.

## Key variants

- **Virilio (1977)**: *Speed and Politics* — speed as political power
- **Stengers (2018)**: *Another Science is Possible* — slow science as response
- **Han (2010)**: *The Burnout Society* — related analysis of performance society

## Known limitations

- Empirical evidence for subjective "pace of life" increase is mixed
- Germany-centric in some respects; universality is contested
- The normative vision of a "good" pace is underdeveloped

## Open problems

- Does AI-accelerated science produce Rosa-style alienation in researchers?
- Stengers's "slow science" manifesto (2018) is a direct response to the acceleration logic Rosa describes

## Relevance to active research

Provides theoretical vocabulary for critiques of AI-driven acceleration in science (DFG 2025, could-ai-slow-science, Stengers 2018). Also relevant to the speed of knowledge production in era of automated research pipelines and AI scientists.
