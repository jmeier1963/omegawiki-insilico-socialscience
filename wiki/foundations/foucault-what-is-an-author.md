## Definition

Foucault's 1969 lecture argues that "the author" is not a natural empirical person but a discursive function — the "author function" — constructed by legal, institutional, and interpretive practices to attribute meaning, ownership, and accountability to texts. This function varies across discourse types and historical periods.

## Intuition

We don't read a text "neutrally" — we attribute it to an author who guarantees its coherence, originality, and accountability. This attribution is a social practice, not a discovery of a natural fact. The author function serves to limit the proliferation of meaning and assign responsibility.

## Formal notation

No formal notation. Key concept:
- **Author function**: the cluster of legal, institutional, and critical operations that constitute "the author" as a discursive figure
- Characteristics: author function is not applied uniformly to all texts; it is linked to ownership and punishment; it enables certain interpretive operations (finding contradictions, establishing intentions); it does not refer to a real individual

## Key variants

- **Barthes (1967)**: "Death of the Author" (companion text — broader deconstructive claim)
- **Legal authorship**: evolving frameworks for AI-generated text

## Known limitations

- Formulated before AI text generation; does not address non-human "authors"
- Primarily a diagnosis, not a normative proposal about what authorship should mean
- The institutional dimension (legal ownership, accountability) is only gestured at

## Open problems

- Does AI have an "author function"? Can AI-generated texts be attributed to an author in Foucault's sense?
- How should academic authorship norms (ICMJE 2023) be revised given AI-generated content?
- Who bears accountability for AI-generated research outputs?

## Relevance to active research

Directly relevant to ICMJE (2023), DFG (2023) authorship debates, and the question of AI as co-author. Also relevant to the Liang et al. (2024) monitoring study — if AI generates text in peer reviews, who is the author-function? Cited in philosophy of AI authorship discussions.
