## Definition

Laudan's 1977 monograph proposes that science is fundamentally a problem-solving activity and that scientific progress should be measured by the effectiveness of research traditions at solving empirical and conceptual problems — without appeal to truth or verisimilitude.

## Intuition

A research tradition is progressive if it solves more problems (including conceptual ones) and generates fewer anomalies than its competitors. This allows rational inter-tradition comparison without invoking incommensurability or requiring truth as the goal.

## Formal notation

No formal notation. Key constructs:
- **Empirical problem**: an item in the world that requires explanation
- **Conceptual problem**: internal inconsistency in a theory, or tension with other accepted beliefs
- **Research tradition**: a broad framework shared by a sequence of theories (broader than Lakatosian programme)
- **Progress**: increase in problem-solving effectiveness relative to competitors

## Key variants

- **Lakatos (1970)**: research programmes (more formalized; requires novel predictions)
- **Kuhn (1962)**: paradigms (sociological; less rational)
- **Laudan (1981)**: *Science and Relativism* — extends the critique of truth-based models

## Known limitations

- "Problem" and "solution" are underdefined — almost any theory can claim some problems solved
- Does not adequately handle cases where conceptual elegance and empirical success conflict
- Ignores the role of experimental constraints and material practices

## Open problems

- How does the problem-solving model apply to AI-driven hypothesis generation (agent-laboratory, AlphaEvolve)?
- Can automated systems identify and solve "conceptual problems" in Laudan's sense?

## Relevance to active research

Useful for evaluating AI-driven science without presupposing truth as a goal. Particularly relevant to applied AI research where practical effectiveness (solved problems) is the criterion, as in Szymanski (2023) and Boiko (2023).
