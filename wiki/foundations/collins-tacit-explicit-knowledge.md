## Definition

Collins's 2010 monograph (Chicago UP) systematically extends Polanyi's concept of tacit knowledge by distinguishing three types: relational tacit knowledge (in principle articulable, context-dependent), somatic tacit knowledge (embodied in the body), and collective tacit knowledge (embedded in social practice and in principle uncodifiable).

## Intuition

Not all tacit knowledge is alike. Some things are tacit merely because we haven't bothered to articulate them. Others are tacit because of how the body works. But the deepest form — collective tacit knowledge — is embedded in the form of life of a community and cannot, even in principle, be fully extracted and codified. This is what formal systems and AI cannot replicate.

## Formal notation

Three-way distinction:
- **Relational tacit knowledge (RTK)**: tacit by context, relationship, or laziness — in principle articulable
- **Somatic tacit knowledge (STK)**: embodied in trained bodily dispositions (riding a bicycle) — hard but possibly articulable
- **Collective tacit knowledge (CTK)**: embedded in social practices; cannot be fully extracted without participating in the form of life

## Key variants

- **Polanyi (1966)**: original "tacit dimension" — "we can know more than we can tell"
- **Collins & Evans (2007)**: *Rethinking Expertise* — interactional vs. contributory expertise
- **Nonaka & Takeuchi (1995)**: knowledge management applications

## Known limitations

- The line between STK and CTK is not always clear
- "Collective tacit knowledge" may be theoretically indistinguishable from what Wittgenstein called "forms of life"
- Some critics argue this triad is not exhaustive

## Open problems

- What forms of collective tacit knowledge does science require, and can AI ever acquire them?
- Ericsson's deliberate practice research is in tension: practice can transfer some somatic tacit knowledge

## Relevance to active research

Central to debates about AI limitations in science: can LLMs acquire collective tacit knowledge? Relevant to Bastani et al. (2024) on learning harm — students offloading to AI may fail to acquire tacit skills. Kosmyna et al. (2025) "cognitive debt" can be framed in terms of failing to develop somatic tacit knowledge.
