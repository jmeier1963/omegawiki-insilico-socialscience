## Definition

Luhmann's 1990 Suhrkamp monograph applies his general theory of autopoietic social systems specifically to science. Science is an operationally closed functional subsystem of modern society whose self-referential medium is truth-claims, coded true/false, and steered by programs (theories and methods).

## Intuition

Science doesn't exist to serve society or to pursue truth because it is natural — it exists because it has differentiated itself as a system that reproduces itself through truth-coded communication. Once differentiated, science has its own logic, immune to direct external steering. External pressure (funding, politics) enters only through environmental perturbations that science filters through its own programs.

## Formal notation

Key constructs:
- **Autopoiesis**: science reproduces itself through its own operations (truth-coded publications, citations, peer review)
- **Binary code**: true / not-true — every scientific communication takes a position on this code
- **Program**: theories and methods that specify how to decide which side of the code applies
- **Structural coupling**: science and other subsystems (economy, politics) influence each other indirectly

## Key variants

- **Merton (1973)**: normative (CUDOS) vs. Luhmann's functional approach
- **Knorr-Cetina (1999)**: empirical epistemic cultures vs. Luhmann's abstract functionalism
- **Stichweh (1984)**: Luhmannian analysis of disciplinary differentiation

## Known limitations

- Highly abstract; difficult to derive empirical predictions
- The autopoiesis metaphor may be strained for social systems
- Does not explain why science produces useful knowledge (the content problem)
- No complete English translation of this book

## Open problems

- Can AI systems participate in the autopoiesis of science? Does AI-generated peer review remain within the truth/not-true code?
- Does AI shift the balance between program-selection (by theories) and structural coupling (by funding/industry)?

## Relevance to active research

Provides a sociological framework for understanding AI's integration into the scientific system. Relevant to DFG (2023/2025) policy on AI authorship and peer review, and to Ahmed et al. (2023) on industrial influence — which reads as structural coupling between the economy and science subsystems.
