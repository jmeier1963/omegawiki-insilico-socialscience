## Definition

Kuhn's 1962 monograph argues that science does not progress by linear accumulation of knowledge. Periods of "normal science" — puzzle-solving within a shared paradigm — are periodically disrupted by crises that lead to "scientific revolutions" in which one paradigm is displaced by an incommensurable successor.

## Intuition

Scientists work within a shared framework of assumptions, methods, and exemplary achievements (the paradigm). Anomalies that the paradigm cannot resolve accumulate until a crisis triggers a gestalt switch to a new paradigm — not by refutation but by community conversion.

## Formal notation

No formal notation; theory is historical-philosophical. Key concepts:
- **Paradigm**: a shared exemplary achievement defining what counts as good science
- **Normal science**: cumulative puzzle-solving within the paradigm
- **Anomaly**: result that resists assimilation into the current paradigm
- **Crisis**: destabilizing accumulation of anomalies
- **Revolution**: displacement of one paradigm by another (incommensurable)

## Key variants

- **Masterman (1970)**: identified 21 distinct senses of "paradigm" in Kuhn's text
- **Lakatos**: refined the paradigm concept into "research programmes" with hard cores and protective belts
- **Feyerabend**: epistemological anarchism as a more radical extension
- **Kuhn (1969 Postscript)**: clarified paradigm → "exemplar" and "disciplinary matrix"

## Known limitations

- "Paradigm" used ambiguously (Masterman critique)
- Incommensurability thesis risks relativism and makes rational theory choice hard to explain
- Historical exemplars (Copernican revolution) are contested
- Struggle to account for continuous micro-progress within normal science

## Open problems

- Does AI-driven science constitute a new paradigm shift?
- How do rapid, overlapping tool changes (LLMs, robotics) map to Kuhnian revolutions?
- Can paradigm-shift frameworks apply to AI models themselves (foundation models vs. specialized)?

## Relevance to active research

Central reference for evaluating claims that AI constitutes a scientific revolution (Chai 2024, Hey 2009). Krauss (2024) empirically tests and largely challenges the paradigm-shift model, finding that <1% of major discoveries are actually abandoned. Messeri & Crockett (2024) implicitly invoke this framework when arguing AI risks producing scientific monocultures.
