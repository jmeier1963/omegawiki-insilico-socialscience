## Definition

Derek de Solla Price's 1963 Columbia UP lectures demonstrate through quantitative analysis that science has grown exponentially since the 17th century (doubling roughly every 10–15 years in publications, personnel, and funding), that this growth cannot continue indefinitely, and that the social organization of science clusters into "invisible colleges" of closely connected researchers.

## Intuition

Science is not a collection of individual geniuses but a large-scale social enterprise with measurable, regular statistical properties. Publication rates, citation patterns, and collaboration networks follow predictable laws. The implications: modern "big science" is qualitatively different from historical "little science."

## Formal notation

Key empirical regularities:
- Exponential growth: S(t) ≈ S₀·e^(αt), doubling time ~10–15 years per domain
- **Lotka's law**: publication productivity follows an inverse-square distribution
- **Price's index**: fraction of references to papers less than 5 years old (measure of "currency" of a field)
- Saturation hypothesis: exponential growth must eventually logistically saturate

## Key variants

- **Merton (1968)**: Matthew Effect on cumulative advantage in science
- **Hirsch (2005)**: h-index as scientometric measure
- **Hicks et al. (2015)**: Leiden Manifesto as normative response to misuse of metrics

## Known limitations

- Quantitative regularities may not generalize across disciplines
- Saturation predictions have been frequently revised upward
- Does not capture qualitative aspects of scientific change

## Open problems

- Does AI-augmented science accelerate exponential growth further or trigger a new kind of saturation?
- How do AI-generated papers affect the statistics Price documented?

## Relevance to active research

Founding text of scientometrics; provides baseline for Hicks et al. (2015) and Gao & Wang (2024) analyses of AI's effect on science metrics. Also relevant to Merton (1968) Matthew Effect debates amplified by AI-driven research acceleration.
