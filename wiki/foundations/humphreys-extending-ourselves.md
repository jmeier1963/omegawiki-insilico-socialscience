## Definition

Humphreys's 2004 Oxford UP monograph argues that computational methods (simulations, Monte Carlo, agent-based models) function like scientific instruments — they extend human cognitive and perceptual capacities beyond what unaided humans can access. This requires revising traditional empiricism: human cognitive abilities can no longer serve as the ultimate epistemic standard when machines systematically surpass them.

## Intuition

The telescope extended vision beyond the naked eye; computational tools extend reasoning beyond unaided cognition. Just as empiricism had to accommodate instruments (you can trust telescopic observations even though humans can't see Neptune directly), it must now accommodate computational tools whose operations humans cannot follow.

## Formal notation

No formal notation. Key argument:
- **Cognitive prosthetics**: computational tools supplement or replace human cognitive operations
- **Template-instantiation epistemology**: computational models are instantiations of abstract templates; their output is evidence just as experimental instruments produce evidence
- **Human-level empiricism is insufficient**: epistemic standards that require a human to check each step are inadequate for modern science

## Key variants

- **Humphreys (2009)**: "Philosophical Novelty of Computer Simulation Methods" (companion paper)
- **Beisbart (2021)**: opacity in simulations (extending Humphreys)
- **Winsberg (2010)**: *Science in the Age of Computer Simulation*

## Known limitations

- Analogy to instruments can be pushed too far — simulations don't straightforwardly detect external phenomena
- Verification of computer models is different from calibrating instruments
- Does not fully address the black-box problem of deep learning

## Open problems

- Does deep learning constitute a qualitatively new form of cognitive extension beyond Humphreys's computational models?
- How should the extended epistemic standards be formulated for AI-assisted science?

## Relevance to active research

Central reference for philosophical treatment of AI as cognitive extension. Builds directly into Beisbart (2021), Boge (2022), Humphreys (2009), and Sullivan (2022) debates about opacity and understanding. Relevant to all AI-science papers.
