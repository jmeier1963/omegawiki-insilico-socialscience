## Definition

Hempel's 1965 essay collection proposes the covering-law (deductive-nomological) model of scientific explanation: an event is explained when it is shown to follow necessarily from a set of true general laws plus initial conditions. The inductive-statistical model covers probabilistic explanations.

## Intuition

To explain why something happened, show that it had to happen (or was likely to happen) given the laws of nature and the circumstances. Explanation is subsumption under a general law.

## Formal notation

Deductive-Nomological (D-N) schema:
```
L₁, L₂, ..., Lₙ    (general laws)
C₁, C₂, ..., Cₘ    (initial conditions)
──────────────────
E                   (explanandum)
```
The inference must be deductively valid; L's must be genuine laws (not just accidental generalizations); C's must be true.

## Key variants

- **D-N model**: deductive subsumption under strict laws
- **Inductive-Statistical (I-S) model**: probabilistic laws + high probability
- **Deductive-Statistical (D-S) model**: deduction from statistical laws
- **Pragmatic theories** (van Fraassen, Achinstein): explanation as context-sensitive contrast

## Known limitations

- Symmetry problem: laws allow "explaining" barometers as causes of storms
- Irrelevance problem: "all men who take birth control pills don't get pregnant"
- Laws are difficult to identify in special sciences (biology, economics)
- Makes no room for mechanistic, causal, or unification accounts

## Open problems

- How do simulation-based and ML-based predictions fit the D-N schema?
- Can opaque AI models explain in the Hempelian sense?
- What replaces the covering-law model in data-driven science (Halevy et al. 2009)?

## Relevance to active research

The standard reference for what scientific explanation requires. Central to debates about whether AI can explain: models like AlphaFold (Jumper 2021) predict without covering laws. Humphreys (2009) and Beisbart (2021) each grapple with how computer simulations relate to Hempelian explanation.
