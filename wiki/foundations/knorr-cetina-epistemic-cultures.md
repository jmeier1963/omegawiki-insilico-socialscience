## Definition

Knorr-Cetina's 1999 monograph (Harvard UP) argues that different sciences have fundamentally different "epistemic cultures" — distinct ways of constructing evidence, organizing social relations, relating theory to experiment, and defining what counts as an object of knowledge. Based on ethnographic fieldwork at CERN (high-energy physics) and molecular biology labs.

## Intuition

"Science" is not one thing. High-energy physics operates as a large-scale collective with elaborate bureaucratic protocols and no individual authorship of experimental results. Molecular biology is artisanal, small-group, opportunistic, and entrepreneurial. These differences run all the way down to what knowledge looks like.

## Formal notation

No formal notation. Two contrasting epistemic cultures:
- **High-energy physics**: "superorganism" collective; highly formal; results are collective outputs; experiments last decades; the "author" is a collaboration of thousands
- **Molecular biology**: artisanal; small groups; opportunistic improvisation; rapid publication cycles; identity bound to individual labs and PIs

## Key variants

- **Galison (1997)**: image/logic trading zones (overlapping focus)
- **Collins (1992)**: sociology of scientific expertise
- **Latour & Woolgar (1979)**: *Laboratory Life* (earlier laboratory studies)

## Known limitations

- Two-culture ethnography may not generalize; more sciences are needed
- Fieldwork done in the 1990s — both communities have changed dramatically
- Risks reifying the two cultures as timeless types

## Open problems

- How does AI change the epistemic cultures of different sciences? Does it homogenize or differentiate them?
- Do AI-driven sciences (computational biology, AI physics) form new epistemic cultures?

## Relevance to active research

Directly relevant to understanding why AI has different impacts in different fields (Schröter 2025, Gao & Wang 2024). Provides vocabulary for discussing the social organization of AI-augmented science and why AI adoption is uneven across disciplines.
