## Definition

Merton's 1973 Chicago UP collection of essays argues that science has a distinctive normative ethos — the CUDOS norms: Communalism (knowledge is a public good), Universalism (claims judged by impersonal criteria), Disinterestedness (scientists act for knowledge not personal gain), Organized Skepticism (claims subjected to communal critique). These norms both sustain and are sustained by the social organization of science.

## Intuition

Science is not just a body of knowledge but a social institution with its own normative structure. These norms explain both science's unusual success at producing reliable knowledge and its characteristic pathologies (priority disputes, fraud) when norms are violated. The norms are functional for the institution, not moral imperatives deduced from ethics.

## Formal notation

**CUDOS norms**:
- **C**ommunalism: scientific findings are a common property of the community; priority claims don't create property rights
- **U**niversalism: claims are evaluated on merit, not the identity (race, nationality, prestige) of the claimant
- **D**isinterestedness: scientists report findings truthfully regardless of personal interest
- **O**rganized Skepticism: suspension of judgment until evidence is in; methodical doubt applied to any claim

Later addition: **Originality** — the primary scientific reward is for novel contribution.

## Key variants

- **Mitroff (1974)**: counter-norms (secrecy, particularism, interested-ness) coexist with CUDOS
- **Longino (1990)**: social epistemology as alternative to normative sociology
- **Merton (1968)**: Matthew Effect — cumulative advantage violates universalism in practice

## Known limitations

- CUDOS describes the ideology of science, not always its practice (fraud, secrecy, bias are common)
- Functionalist framing explains norms by their effects, not their causes
- Does not engage with the content of science (only its social structure)

## Open problems

- Do AI systems undermine CUDOS norms? E.g., does AI-generated content violate disinterestedness if AI has training objectives?
- Industrial AI research (Ahmed et al. 2023) systematically violates communalism (closed models) and disinterestedness (commercial objectives)

## Relevance to active research

Canonical reference for evaluating whether AI-driven science threatens the normative foundations of science. Ahmed et al. (2023), Strathern (1997), and Smith (2006) all implicitly address Mertonian norm violations. Directly relevant to DFG (2023/2025) policy documents on AI integrity.
