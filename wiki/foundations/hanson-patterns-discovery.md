## Definition

Hanson's 1958 work argues that scientific observation is always theory-laden: what scientists perceive is not raw sense-data but structured experience shaped by prior theoretical commitments. The book also analyses abductive (retroductive) reasoning as the logic of scientific discovery.

## Intuition

Tycho Brahe and Kepler watching the same sunrise see different things — not merely interpret the same data differently. Seeing is not a passive reception of information but an active, theory-structured act. Discovery therefore requires prior conceptual frameworks.

## Formal notation

No formal notation; argument is via philosophical analysis and historical examples. Key claim: "There is a sense in which Tycho and Kepler do not see the same thing in the east at dawn." Perception is propositionally structured (seeing-as, Wittgenstein's duck-rabbit).

## Key variants

- **Kuhn**: extends theory-ladenness to the full paradigm level
- **Feyerabend**: uses theory-ladenness to argue against observation as theory-neutral arbiter
- **Fodor (1983)**: argues perception is modular and encapsulated — challenges strong theory-ladenness

## Known limitations

- Theory-ladenness is a matter of degree; not all observation is equally theory-laden
- Conflates the context of discovery with the context of justification
- Abductive logic of discovery has been challenged (discovery = no logic?)

## Open problems

- Does AI-driven pattern detection in data constitute "observation"? Is it theory-laden?
- Can AI systems trained on data make abductive discoveries without prior theories?

## Relevance to active research

Foundational for arguments about what AI can and cannot "discover." Duede (2023) and Alvarado (2022) implicitly engage with theory-ladenness when arguing that ML models function as detection instruments. Anderson's (2008) "end of theory" claim is a direct inversion of Hanson's thesis.
