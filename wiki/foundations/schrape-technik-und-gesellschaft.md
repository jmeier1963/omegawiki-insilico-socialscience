## Definition

Jan-Felix Schrape's 2025 Reclam Verlag introductory monograph (*Technik und Gesellschaft: Eine kurze Einführung*, 181 pp., ISBN 9783150146767) traces how technological change — from electricity and railways to smartphones and AI — is always co-constituted with social structures, power relations, and cultural contexts. Steering technological development requires understanding this mutual shaping.

## Intuition

Technology is not an autonomous force that impacts society from outside. Nor is it purely a social construction. Instead, technology and society co-evolve: technologies shape social practices, and social practices shape which technologies are developed, adopted, and used in what ways. The history of electricity, the car, and the internet all show this mutual constitution.

## Formal notation

No formal notation. Introductory text in the Reclams Universal-Bibliothek series — accessible sociology of technology overview. Key orienting perspectives covered:
- Social construction of technology (SCOT)
- Actor-network theory (Latour)
- Large technical systems (Hughes)
- System-theoretical approaches (Luhmann/Stichweh)

## Key variants

- **Winner (1986)**: "Do Artifacts Have Politics?" — classic SCOT-adjacent essay
- **Jasanoff (2004)**: co-production of science and social order
- **Luhmann (1990)**: science system as social system (foundational)

## Known limitations

- Introductory text — necessarily selective and non-original in its theoretical claims
- 2025 publication; treatment of AI is fresh but cannot engage with very recent developments

## Open problems

- How does the co-constitution perspective apply to AI? What social orders does AI co-produce?

## Relevance to active research

Background reading for understanding the social embeddedness of AI technologies. Provides sociological vocabulary for the AI-in-science debates, particularly for understanding why AI adoption is uneven and how institutional contexts shape AI's effects.
