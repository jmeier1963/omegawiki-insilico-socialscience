## Definition

Vogl's 2021 C.H. Beck monograph (no complete English translation) argues in three theses: (1) platform capitalism (Amazon, Google, Uber) is the latest metamorphosis of financialized capital in which information management is the core value source; (2) the merger of financial logic and communication infrastructure creates new forms of power; (3) an "affect economy" of resentment is cultivated by platform capitalism to sustain dominance.

## Intuition

Platforms don't just sell goods or services — they sell communication infrastructure and monetize attention and affect. Resentment (envy, grievance, powerlessness) is particularly valuable: it keeps users engaged, drives viral sharing, and fragments solidarity. This is not a side effect of platform capitalism but structurally produced by it.

## Formal notation

No formal notation. Three theses:
- **Thesis 1 (Financial capitalism)**: Platform firms are continuations of financialized capital from the 1970s, now using information as the primary asset
- **Thesis 2 (Power/Communication)**: The merger of financial and communicational logic generates new forms of distributed, opaque power over public spheres
- **Thesis 3 (Affect economy)**: Resentment is actively cultivated as a profitable form of social engagement

## Key variants

- **Srnicek (2016)**: *Platform Capitalism* — related economic analysis
- **Zuboff (2019)**: *Surveillance Capitalism* — complementary account
- **Han (2017)**: *In the Swarm* — adjacent analysis of digital public spheres

## Known limitations

- No full English translation — limited accessibility
- The "affect economy" thesis is empirically underdeveloped
- Three-thesis structure is ambitious but potentially underdeveloped in each

## Open problems

- Does AI-driven content generation amplify the affect economy of resentment?
- What are the implications for AI in public discourse and scientific communication?

## Relevance to active research

Background for understanding the political economy of AI platforms and their effects on epistemic practices. Relevant to debates about AI bias (Xu 2025), and to questions about incentive structures in AI-driven knowledge production.
