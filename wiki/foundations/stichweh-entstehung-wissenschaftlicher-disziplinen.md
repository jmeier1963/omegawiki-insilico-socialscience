## Definition

Rudolf Stichweh's 1984 Suhrkamp doctoral thesis (full title: *Zur Entstehung des modernen Systems wissenschaftlicher Disziplinen: Physik in Deutschland 1740–1890*) uses Luhmannian systems theory and the history of German physics to argue that modern scientific disciplines emerged through functional differentiation: each discipline established operational closure, a characteristic object domain, and institutional self-reproduction through university chairs and journals.

## Intuition

Disciplines didn't form because ideas naturally clustered together. They formed because social systems — universities, journals, professional associations — developed that reproduced specific research practices over time. Physics became "physics" not when it had a coherent theory but when it had institutionally differentiated forms of communication, career tracks, and object domains.

## Formal notation

No formal notation. Key Luhmannian constructs applied to disciplines:
- **Operational closure**: a discipline reproduces itself through its own communication operations
- **Object domain**: the specific type of phenomena a discipline claims exclusive competence over
- **Structural coupling**: disciplines interact with university structures, funding, and other disciplines
- **Differentiation**: the process by which disciplines separate from a previously undifferentiated learned discourse

English-language companion: Stichweh, "The Sociology of Scientific Disciplines," *Science in Context* 5(1), 1992.

## Key variants

- **Luhmann (1990)**: science as a whole (Stichweh applies the model to sub-disciplinary level)
- **Knorr-Cetina (1999)**: epistemic cultures at the discipline level
- **Galison (1997)**: trading zones between disciplines

## Known limitations

- No complete English translation — primarily accessible to German readers or via the 1992 article
- Historical case study (German physics) — generalization requires care
- Highly abstract Luhmannian vocabulary

## Open problems

- Is AI-driven science creating new disciplines or disrupting existing disciplinary structures?
- How do AI/ML affect the operational closure of scientific disciplines?

## Relevance to active research

Provides the sociological foundation for understanding disciplinary formation and dissolution — relevant to debates about whether "AI science" is becoming a discipline, and why AI adoption varies by field (Gao & Wang 2024, Schröter 2025).
