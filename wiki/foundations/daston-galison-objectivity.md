## Definition

Daston and Galison's 2007 study (Zone Books) uses the history of scientific atlases to argue that "objectivity" is not a timeless epistemic value but a historically specific one that emerged ca. 1860. It succeeded the earlier ideal of "truth-to-nature" and has itself been partly superseded by "trained judgment."

## Intuition

What it means to be an objective scientist has changed historically. 18th-century naturalists idealized the "typical" specimen (truth-to-nature, guided by reason). 19th-century scientists developed mechanical objectivity — suppressing the willful self to let instruments and mechanical recording speak. 20th-century scientists added trained judgment — the expert eye that knows what to ignore in raw images.

## Formal notation

No formal notation. Three epistemic regimes:
- **Truth-to-nature** (~1750–1860): ideal type over individual variation; reason structures observation
- **Mechanical objectivity** (~1860–1950): suppress subjective interference; instruments record "nature itself"
- **Trained judgment** (~1950–): expert pattern recognition that goes beyond mechanical recording

## Key variants

- **Porter (1995)**: *Trust in Numbers* — quantification as objectivity in social sciences
- **Shapin & Schaffer (1985)**: *Leviathan and the Air-Pump* — experimental objectivity as social construction

## Known limitations

- Focuses on visual science (atlases); may not generalize to other domains
- Periodization is schematic; all three regimes coexist in most periods
- Underspecifies how the transition between regimes occurs

## Open problems

- Which epistemic regime does AI-assisted science belong to? Is deep learning a new form of "trained judgment"?
- Does automated pattern recognition constitute a fourth regime of objectivity?

## Relevance to active research

Highly relevant to debates about AI as a scientific tool. Messeri & Crockett (2024) implicitly invoke Daston/Galison when worrying about AI eroding the trained judgment that normally produces understanding. Krenn et al. (2022) engage with what AI-assisted "understanding" means.
